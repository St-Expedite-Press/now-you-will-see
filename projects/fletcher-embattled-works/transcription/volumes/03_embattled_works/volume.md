## Volume Argument

The embattled period is not a name for failure, and it is not only a name for
difficulty with critics. It is a triple war: Fletcher against the Imagist label
that had been useful but had now become a cage; Fletcher against the pull of
the South, which he could feel closing in before he was willing to call it
home; and Fletcher against himself — a war that resolves, eventually, in the
late collapse back to Arkansas and the falling works.

_Breakers and Granite_ begins the Southern return before the official late
phase. _Parables_ and _Branches of Adam_ are the ambitious, unwieldy,
underread books that keep the middle Fletcher from becoming a postcard of
Imagism. _The Black Rock_ is the embattled closing text, the last stand before
the resolution begins.

This volume solves the physical problem of the old dominant/falling split: the
1921-1928 books are neither purely imagist nor purely Southern regionalist, and
they have resisted both labels long enough to deserve their own container.
