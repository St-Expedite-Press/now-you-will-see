## Original Lineation

Within the steep and steel-blue dome of sky
  There is no stir nor murmur, swift or soft:
  The sun bears steadily his torch aloft,
Striding up to the zenith, where on high
  The sleepless God whose worshipper he is
Watches another day flash past in flame:
A day whose glory, worship, perfect fame,
  Are but to breathe in an immobile bliss.

Now bees and brutes and men leave drink and
    food;
Sunk in a mighty reverie the earth,
Teeming with passionate joy, perfected pain,
Wings on its tireless path so oft pursued,
Through day and night, through death and life
    and birth,
Through suns supreme and thunder-showers of
    rain.

## Context Notes

Note: Her I've chosen to embolden the poem’s underlying sonnet form by resolving the original print’s line wraps into full metrical lines. This is clearly a sonnet, but I've broken a messy octave into two quatrains. A harder than normal decision because it involves makeing explicit the implied upward through sky, sun, zenith, God, and flame. The sestet turns at “Now,” descending to bees, brutes, men, and the earth’s noon-stilled reverie. This lineation emphasizes the poem’s central paradox: beneath apparent immobility, the world continues its tireless motion through day, night, death, life, birth, sun, and rain.
