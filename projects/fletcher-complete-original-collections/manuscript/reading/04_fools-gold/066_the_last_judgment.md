## Original Lineation

Methought I saw the final Judgment Day:
God woke from slumber, forth He swiftly thrust
His lightnings: earth and sea were burned away.
All souls arose, like a great cloud of dust.

Then from His all-enduring throne He spake:
"The honest bodies of beasts, I gladly bless.
I give to them, from ape to crawling snake,
Immortal lengths of bestial happiness.

"Man, man alone, half-body and half-soul,
Half-wise, half-fool, half-dawning and half-night,
Him I condemn, both separately and whole,
The bottomless abyss remove him from My sight!"

## Context Notes

Three Petrarchan quatrains (ABAB CDCD EFEF), the poem presents a vision of the Last Judgment (the Christian eschatological event described in Matthew 25, Revelation 20, and elaborated in centuries of theology and art) with a Swiftian twist: God blesses the beasts -- 'from ape to crawling snake' -- and condemns humanity. The Last Judgment tradition in Western art runs from Giotto's Arena Chapel (c.1305) through Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel (1536-1541), always placing the blessed on the right and the damned on the left; Fletcher's version eliminates the blessed human souls entirely, replacing them with animals. The phrase 'Man, man alone, half-body and half-soul, / Half-wise, half-fool, half-dawning and half-night' is a precise enumeration of the divided nature that Blaise Pascal analysed in the *Pensées* (1670) as the condition of humanity between beast and angel; here the division is a sentence of condemnation rather than a call to aspiration.
