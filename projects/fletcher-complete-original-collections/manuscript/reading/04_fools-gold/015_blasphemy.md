## Original Lineation

Beauty and power and might
To Thee are hateful blight:

The tiger has all three,
So chained by man must be:

A wretched ape half skinned,
Diseased and base of mind,

Now hast Thou set above
Creatures of health and love!

Cattle and dogs allowed
This sway: as slaves they bowed

Their heads to Thy decree,
But the tiger yet is free.

O Thou, Who hast Thy Will
Through torture, threat and ill;

I, pitiful babbling mite,
Thou "lovest, yet dost smite,"

I ask the tiger's wage:
Grant me the bars, the cage,

The misery, and the fate,
So that I free may hate

Thee and Thy Works for aye:
So that I free may die,

Not an enlightened clod,
But a brute without a God.

Take back then what was given:
No soul I want, no heaven!

For I'd be rather naught
Than in Thy meshes caught,

The last slave of Thy plan,
Thy vilest creature, man!

## Context Notes

Fourteen couplets in tetrameter, rhyming AA throughout. l. 27 ('But the tiger yet is free') echoes Blake's 'The Tyger' (*Songs of Experience*, 1794), which asks what force dared frame the tiger's fearful symmetry; here the tiger becomes the emblem of beauty and power that God has subjected to chains.
