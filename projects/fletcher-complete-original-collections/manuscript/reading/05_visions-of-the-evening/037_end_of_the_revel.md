## Original Lineation

The torches sputter out,
    Smoke hangs about the street;
    Now, loosely linked, a rout
Sways by with stumbling feet.

They pass, these stragglers lost.--
    Suddenly bangs a door;
A sot, in gutter tossed,
    Breathes out a raucous snore.

The swaying cressets bright
    Burn through their chains: they fall,
And black pools quench their light:
    While darkness shakes her shawl.

Out of a corner she
    Catlike begins to creep;
Afar, the echoes die;
    The shadows crouch and leap.

Griefs in new strength return:
    Coldly watching our cares
Like eyes that freeze and burn,
    Come back the lofty stars.

## Context Notes

Five ballad quatrains in ABAB form, the poem is a miniature nocturne depicting the aftermath of an evening's entertainment as the last revellers disperse and darkness reclaims the street. 'Cressets' (iron baskets mounted on poles and filled with burning pitch or oil to light outdoor spaces) are an archaic or theatrical form of street lighting -- by the Edwardian period, gas and electric lamps had replaced them in cities; their appearance here gives the setting a deliberately timeless, historical quality. 'Darkness shakes her shawl' is a brief but precise personification: darkness as an old woman wrapping herself in her garment to settle for the night. The final stanza's return of the stars as 'eyes that freeze and burn' watching human griefs is a characteristic Symbolist inversion: the natural world observes and judges, remaining cold while human feeling burns.
