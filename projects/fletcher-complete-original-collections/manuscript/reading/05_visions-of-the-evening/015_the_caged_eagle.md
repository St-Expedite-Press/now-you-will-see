## Original Lineation

He sits upon his perch in the far evening:
    Motionless, watching twilight fade away.
His wings he never rustles, he never makes
        a sound,
He waits until the night devours the day.

And then he shuts his eyes, still poised deliberate
    Upon his perch, nor moves throughout the night,
He dreams, before the morning brings him waking,
    That bars have vanished 'twixt him and the light.

He sits upon his perch in the fresh morning:
    Interrogating silently the sky.
No eyelid moves, no feather, as I watch him:--
    I also know the bars more strong than I!

## Context Notes

Three quatrains in ABCB; the editorial relineation resolves a line broken by the original typesetting.

ll. 18–19 ('He dreams, before the morning brings him waking, / That bars have vanished 'twixt him and the light'): The eagle's single consolation is a compensatory dream that does not survive waking.

l. 24 ('I also know the bars more strong than I!'): The observer identifies with the observed in the final line; compare the mirror-identification in poem 005, where Solitude's unveiled face is the speaker's own.
