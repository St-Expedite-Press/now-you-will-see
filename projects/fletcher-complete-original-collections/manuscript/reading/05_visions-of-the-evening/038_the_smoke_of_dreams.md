## Original Lineation

My soul is as one who climbs a shadowy peak
And sees below, through the half-revealed
    haze of the plains,
The while his thoughts dream to rid themselves of
    their dream,
Slim ropes of smoke that sway, 'twixt the earth and
    sky.

In the dim Autumn peace quite motionlessly
I watch the smoke of a million of years gone by:
These are dead and forgotten, a dream of yesterday,
An empty rhyme is all the tomb they own.

I know not why or how I wait there alone;
But numberless and numberless, I see
Only against the vague sky, the vague streams
Of pale, pale, passing smoke, smoke of my dreams.

## Context Notes

Three irregular quatrains with an editorial relineation, the poem presents the speaker on an Autumnal hillside watching 'the smoke of a million years gone by' -- the histories of forgotten lives -- rise as thin ropes against the sky. The title's 'smoke of dreams' combines two familiar Romantic and Symbolist figures: the evanescence of smoke (which rises and disperses without trace) and the unreality of dreams. 'An empty rhyme is all the tomb they own' applies the lyric poem itself as a tombstone substitute: the unnamed dead are preserved only in a rhyme, which is both empty (without substance) and all they have. 'Pale, pale, passing smoke, smoke of my dreams' -- the doubled 'pale' and the repeated 'smoke' in the final line enact the obsessive returning of the image, and the shift from 'the smoke of a million years gone by' to 'smoke of my dreams' in the closing line collapses the historical vision into the speaker's own self-elegy.
