## Original Lineation

I set my soul upon nothing,
    I counted all things as dross:--
And I am desolate and weary
As a grey albatross.

All land I left to the northward;
    And now I keep my flight
Forever, over billows
    That change not, day nor night.

At times a reef surf-battered
    Affords me scanty stay,
And a derelict, half-sunken,
    I once met, in my way.

Now, 'mid a sea uncharted,
    Under the Southern Cross,
Towards the last ice-barrier,
    I move, an albatross.

## Context Notes

Four quatrains in ABCB ballad form, the poem engages directly with Baudelaire's 'L'Albatros' (*Les Fleurs du Mal* II), which gives the albatross to the poet as his emblem of grounded greatness: 'Ses ailes de géant l'empêchent de marcher' (his giant's wings keep him from walking).

ll. 25–28: Fletcher's revision is geographic rather than emblematic — his albatross is neither mocked on deck nor shot, but flies under the Southern Cross towards 'the last ice-barrier,' navigating away from human society rather than suffering within it.
