## Original Lineation

As flowers smell sweeter, having been wet by
    the rain,
So my thoughts pour forth in the damp
still night their song:
And the clean fresh perfume of their inextinguish-
    able pain,
Seems to pass from the earth and to suffer no
    wrong.

And my thoughts are of mouldering poems, left half-
    unexpressed,
Desires which I felt long ago, I know not why:
They are crumbled to dust like flowers a long while
    pressed,
Yet they still preserve the faint sweetness of love
    gone by.

Green gardens they filled: now they fall one by one,
Upon the dark roads that lead ever outward far:
The flowers of youth, green-white as a frozen star,
The flowers of sorrow, dead ere their spring was
    done.

## Editorial Relineation

As flowers smell sweeter, having been wet by the rain,
So my thoughts pour forth in the damp
still night their song:
And the clean fresh perfume of their inextinguishable pain,
Seems to pass from the earth and to suffer no wrong.

And my thoughts are of mouldering poems, left half-unexpressed,
Desires which I felt long ago, I know not why:
They are crumbled to dust like flowers a long while pressed,
Yet they still preserve the faint sweetness of love gone by.

Green gardens they filled: now they fall one by one,
Upon the dark roads that lead ever outward far:
The flowers of youth, green-white as a frozen star,
The flowers of sorrow, dead ere their spring was done.

## Context Notes

Three quatrains in ABAB; the editorial relineation resolves a run-on line in the original typesetting.

ll. 1–4: The opening stanza reverses the Symbolist conceit of rain as melancholy (cf. Verlaine's 'Il pleure dans mon cœur'): here rain intensifies the perfume of thoughts, making pain a sweetener.

l. 11 ('green-white as a frozen star'): A visual simile for the colour of early spring buds seen against a cold sky.
