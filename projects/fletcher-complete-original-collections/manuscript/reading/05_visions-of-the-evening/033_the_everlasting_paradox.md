## Original Lineation

Nothing is more than all things great.
From the fate of chance comes the chance
    of fate:
From the life of song comes the song of life:
From the strife of wrong, the wrong of strife:
From the earth the flower, from flower earth,
From birth the power, from power birth.

## Editorial Relineation

Nothing is more than all things great.
From the fate of chance comes the chance of fate:
From the life of song comes the song of life:
From the strife of wrong, the wrong of strife:
From the earth the flower, from flower earth,
From birth the power, from power birth.

## Context Notes

Six lines in three chiastic couplets, the poem is a compressed philosophical aphorism-sequence in which each line reverses the previous by exchanging subject and predicate. 'The fate of chance' and 'the chance of fate' are logically interchangeable yet semantically different: fate implies necessity, chance implies contingency, and the reversal suggests that fate and chance are mutually constituted. The poem's opening 'Nothing is more than all things great' asserts an ontological equality between nothing and the totality of things -- a formulation close to the Taoist wu (*non-being* as the ground of all being) and to Buddhist *śūnyatā*. The earth/flower and birth/power reversals enact the cyclical structure of the poem's argument: all processes return to their opposites without resolution. The poem functions as a kind of Zen koan embedded in the collection's final sequence.
