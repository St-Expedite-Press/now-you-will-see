## Original Lineation

The half of this world's story is a blank,
    For half is hidden in the gulf of night.
    No one has written and no one can write
What happened then, how feasted, ate and drank
Men in unechoing and empty halls
    Whereto they all have wended countless years.
    How fared it there where deeds and hopes and fears
Are but forgotten dreams with light footfalls?

We make our history and our heroes' lives
    From vast and petty notions of the day:
How in the sunlight each man fails or thrives.
    But we forget how night and sleep betray
Sunlight and sorrow, life and birth and death,
To be alike vain and a waste of breath.

## Context Notes

A Petrarchan sonnet (ABBAABBA CDCDEE), one of the most formally regular poems in *Visions of the Evening*, the poem argues that history records only the daylit half of human experience -- 'half is hidden in the gulf of night.' The octave imagines the unrecorded night-time lives of historical people: their feasting, their halls, their forgotten dreams. The sestet then makes the wider argument: we construct history from 'vast and petty notions of the day' (the sunlit, public record) while ignoring night and sleep, which equally constitute human life. The final couplet's claim that 'night and sleep betray / Sunlight and sorrow, life and birth and death, / To be alike vain and a waste of breath' places all human activity -- sunlit or nocturnal -- in the category of vanity, an Ecclesiastes-style levelling that is the poem's surprising conclusion.
