## Original Lineation

(June 17, 1910)

Be my grave on the mountain side
    Where never a man goes by;
For of all that have lived and died,
    None strove against man as I:
In churchyards trim and well-kept
    Sleep the cowardly mob of the mart:
Let me sleep, as in life I slept,
    Next Nature's granite heart.

Be my ashes scattered in snow
    That has lain for a thousand years;
Be my flame then at last quenched. No,
    No honour for me, nor tears!
The rain will beat through the night,
    And the wind will howl in the morn,
Like the rhymes and cries I have torn
    From my heart with desperate might.

## Context Notes

Two ballad octets in ABABCDDC form, dated June 17, 1910, the poem is the companion to the closing poem 040, 'My Monument,' together forming a diptych on the poet's posthumous fate. The dated inscription is rare in the collection and gives the poem a documentary quality -- an actual moment of composition. The request to be buried on a mountain side, 'next Nature's granite heart,' explicitly rejects the 'churchyards trim and well-kept' where 'the cowardly mob of the mart' sleep, asserting the speaker's difference from bourgeois society even in death. The second stanza's request to have ashes 'scattered in snow / That has lain for a thousand years' returns to the high-Alpine imagery of *Fools' Gold* (the Great St. Bernard Pass, the Grande Chartreuse). 'The rhymes and cries I have torn / From my heart with desperate might' -- the comparison of the wind's howl to the poet's own creative struggle -- gives wind its conventional Romantic significance as the breath of inspiration (Shelley, 'Ode to the West Wind').
