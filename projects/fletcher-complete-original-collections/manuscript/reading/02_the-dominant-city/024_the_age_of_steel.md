## Original Lineation

It was the age of iron,
Till men built furnaces
By many a silent vale,--
'Tis now the age of steel!

The great grey frameworks rise
Upwards to lowering skies,
The drills and hammers roar:
Tunnels sink, low and dull,
Earth, sea, and air are full
With shapes that leap and soar.
It is the age of steel.

The curse that was laid on man
Put God, too, 'neath its ban,
And He is dead thereof:
To break the spell of fate,
Let mankind toil and hate
Quitting the dream of love.

It is the age of steel;
Let my song, then, be a sword!
It cuts to my heart, but it pierces your own as well,
Yes, it pierces all hearts from Heaven down to hell.
Welcome, the age of steel!

## Context Notes

The hymn to industrial force as poetic subject reaches Fletcher from Emile Verhaeren, whose *Les Villes tentaculaires* (1895) had made furnaces and factories epic. Fletcher's turn comes in ll. 31–32: 'Let my song, then, be a sword! / It cuts to my heart, but it pierces your own as well' — the song does not celebrate the steel but becomes it, and the sword cuts the singer first.

Strophes of four, seven, six, and five three-beat lines, with the refrain 'It is the age of steel' recurring at irregular intervals; the final strophe stretches into long lines as the song becomes the sword.
