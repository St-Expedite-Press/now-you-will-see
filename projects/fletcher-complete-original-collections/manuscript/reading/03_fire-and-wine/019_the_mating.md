## Original Lineation

I am content to take you as you are,
  As such I think you answer every test.
I'll seek no more, for I have wandered far
  And you are only woman, like the rest.

As well as any other will you do,
  As ill as any other you were made.
I am not much to boast on too, you know.
  Ask little, then, and let it be a trade.

Though love's a soiled and hard and withered thing
  Cast on a rubbish-heap of worn-out days,
To bear the rains and bleak winds of the spring:
  'Tis all we have, 'tis all we need to praise.

I'll look down in the cellars of my heart
  For those old casks, long-broached, of love and hate,
Perhaps some lees are left: if so, take part.
  Such wedding-feast does fortune set, O mate!

## Context Notes

Four ABAB quatrains presenting love as commercial transaction — l. 8 ('Ask little, then, and let it be a trade'). The title reduces love to biology.

ll. 13–16: 'old casks, long-broached, of love and hate' with their 'lees' (the sediment at the bottom of a wine barrel) apply the *Fire and Wine* title-register to love's leftovers. The anti-romantic deflationary stance belongs to the tradition of Swift and Rochester.
