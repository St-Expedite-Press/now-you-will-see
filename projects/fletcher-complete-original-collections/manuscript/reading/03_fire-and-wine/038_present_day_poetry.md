## Original Lineation

But yesterday the poets
  Ere they could dare to sing,
Must find the one thing needful;
  A mighty voice to ring:

But we have changed that evil,
  And all is now once more
As if no voice attempted
  In song to rise and soar.

To-day, we have no singers:
  For our doting eyes are cast
On two colossal phantoms,
  The future, and the past.

In the cause of reform and progress
  We have burnt to-day at the stake;
She may pray or she may shudder,
  For the past, or the future's sake.

If you would be a poet,
  These words will save you strife:
The one, sole thing you need is
  A philosophy of life!

## Context Notes

Five ballad quatrains (ABCB) with a pointed satirical argument: contemporary poetry has lost its 'mighty voice' because it is paralysed between two 'colossal phantoms' -- the future (progressive ideology) and the past (tradition) -- while sacrificing the present ('We have burnt to-day at the stake'). The closing stanza's ironic prescription -- 'The one, sole thing you need is / A philosophy of life!' -- deflates the Victorian tendency (Carlyle, Matthew Arnold, later Shaw) to demand that poets supply moral or social programmes, reducing it to a slogan. The poem belongs to a tradition of satirical ars poetica poems that include Pope's mock-prescriptions in *An Essay on Criticism* (1711) and the Romantic reaction against didacticism; Fletcher's position is closer to the Symbolist insistence that poetry is not philosophy or reform.
