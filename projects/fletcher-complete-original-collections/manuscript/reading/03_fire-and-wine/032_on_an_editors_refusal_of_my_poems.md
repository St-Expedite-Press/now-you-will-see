## Original Lineation

Who knows in acid-bitten bronze to scrape
  His thought he need not fear to die unknown:
His immortality he cannot 'scape;
  He rests, as rests the steel-grey granite stone.

Resisting shallow change, resisting death,
  His song pours ever into earth unheard;
The great sigh of the myriads taking breath,
  He knows it his: and that is his reward.

Not his to count the chink of coins, or worse,
  To waste his time in remedying Fame's wrong;
For all the wild force of the universe
  Bursts from his soul, shaking his strings to song.

## Context Notes

A twelve-line poem in three Petrarchan quatrains (ABBA ABBA CDD), presenting the rejected poet's defiance through an extended engraving and masonry metaphor: 'acid-bitten bronze' refers to etching, the printmaker's technique of using acid to bite an image into metal, and the resulting print cannot be erased; 'steel-grey granite stone' similarly figures the work as geologically durable, resisting 'shallow change.' The poem belongs to a tradition of poets writing adversarially about editors and publishers -- the companion piece 033 is addressed to 'The Publisher Who Refused to Publish My Poems' -- and the two poems together form a small sequence of artistic self-assertion. 'Fame's wrong' in the third quatrain treats Fame as a capricious power that has erred in neglecting the speaker; the resolution is Stoic indifference: 'for all the wild force of the universe / Bursts from his soul, shaking his strings to song,' aligning the poet's energy with cosmic rather than commercial forces.
