## Original Lineation

He is one drunken with monotonous wine
Of rhyme divine and undivine:
Who seeing all things scarcely shows
He sees to the end of his own nose:
Who hates the thing he loves, and makes
A passion of the thing he hates:
Who in the mountains seeks the plain:
And in the sun desires the rain:
Who flashes on life's evil flood
Transient gleams of joyful good:
Who lives in the north, and loves the south,
And gainsays the song from his own mouth.
Life is his death and death may be
More of life than he ever may see;
Hell is his Heaven: should he dwell
In Heaven itself, 'twould be his Hell.

## Context Notes

'Who hates the thing he loves, and makes / A passion of the thing he hates' is the oldest paradox in the personal lyric: Catullus 85 -- 'Odi et amo. Quare id faciam, fortasse requiris. / Nescio, sed fieri sentio et excrucior' (I hate and I love. Why, you may ask. I do not know; I feel it happen, and am racked). Fletcher multiplies the two-term torment into a whole catalogue -- mountains and plain, sun and rain, north and south, Heaven and Hell -- as if the Catullan knot, tied once, could be tied down every line of a self.
