## Original Lineation

Poets can only hear,
  So they glasses wear;
Painters can only see,
  Deaf they always are;
Sculptors can only feel,
  So they have no mind;
Musicians can only sing,
  They are deaf and dumb and blind.

## Context Notes

Eight lines in a simple ABAB scheme, the poem presents the four arts (poetry, painting, sculpture, music) as constituted by sensory privation: poets can only hear (so they wear glasses, compensating with sight); painters can only see (so they are deaf); sculptors can only feel (so they have no mind); musicians can only sing (so they are 'deaf and dumb and blind'). The argument is that artistic specialisation is also a form of impairment -- each art pays for its particular sensitivity with the loss of others. The epigrammatic compression and paradoxical structure recall Oscar Wilde's aphorisms and the Decadent tradition of reducing aesthetic argument to compact formula; the poem functions almost as a satirical coda to the larger cluster of ars poetica poems surrounding it.
