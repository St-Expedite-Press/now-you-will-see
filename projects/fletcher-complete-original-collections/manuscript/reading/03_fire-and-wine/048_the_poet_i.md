## Original Lineation

I am a poet.--Dusky image wrought
After long years within the fires of thought:
Leaving my ancient dross to feed the mire,
And offering up my pure gold to the fire.

Alike to those who tread my vestiture,
  And those who wipe its stain, I am serene:
  I know the meaning of each strange, wild scene;
And every chasm cross with footstep sure.

I am a poet and a mystery,
Each day myself as in a glass I see:
Creator and created fused in one,
Sun that makes night and night that drinks the sun.

## Context Notes

Twelve lines in three quatrains (AABB / ABBA / AABB), the poem opens with a declarative 'I am a poet' and develops the identity through three stages: first, the alchemical transformation of dross to gold ('leaving my ancient dross to feed the mire, / And offering up my pure gold to the fire'); second, Stoic equanimity toward critics and admirers alike; third, self-reflexive mystery ('Creator and created fused in one, / Sun that makes night and night that drinks the sun'). 'Dross' is the waste material or slag left when metal is refined, and 'pure gold' is the refined artistic product; the metallurgical metaphor links to poem 026 ('I am the smith of words'). The final couplet's paradox -- sun making night and night drinking sun -- draws on alchemical imagery of the *coincidentia oppositorum* (union of opposites), a philosophical concept associated with Heraclitus and later with mystical traditions (Nicholas of Cusa, Jacob Böhme) in which contrary principles resolve into unity.
