## Original Lineation

I would not sing one song, but many a strain
  expressing
The steeps of the sky and the lowest depths of
  the gutter.
I would put all the tones this world can utter
Into my voice, the rhythm proudly stressing
Until the stars stood still to listen to that song,
Ever ascending afresh, new and sublime and strong.

I would not be a single string low-throbbing
  To a few sick souls in a lamplit hospital staying,
But the great harp of the world, shaking with
  laughter and sobbing,
Under the sky to all the wind's wild playing.
If my strings soon break, what matter? the Maker
  of song
May stretch then some other strings than mine; more
  new, more strong!

## Editorial Relineation

I would not sing one song, but many a strain expressing

  To a few sick souls in a lamplit hospital staying,
But the great harp of the world, shaking with laughter and sobbing,
Under the sky to all the wind's wild playing.
If my strings soon break, what matter? the Maker of song
May stretch then some other strings than mine; more new, more strong!

## Context Notes

Two twelve-line stanzas in loose hexameter couplets, presenting the poet's ambition in explicitly orchestral terms. The rejection of being 'a single string low-throbbing / To a few sick souls in a lamplit hospital' -- with its echo of late-Victorian vers-de-société and drawing-room verse -- in favour of being 'the great harp of the world, shaking with laughter and sobbing, / Under the sky to all the wind's wild playing' articulates a Whitmanesque democratic sublime: poetry as cosmic instrument rather than intimate consolation. The Maker of Song (capitalised) in the penultimate couplet is ambiguous -- it could be God, or nature, or the impersonal creative force that will replace the individual poet when his strings break; the resignation 'If my strings soon break, what matter?' echoes Keats's acceptance of mortal limitation in 'Ode to a Nightingale' but reorients the sentiment toward impersonal rather than personal continuity. The poem belongs to the ars poetica cluster that dominates Book II: Wine (poems 26-51).
