## Original Lineation

In the valleys there is balm:
  And yet the valleys seem
To me devoid of calm.
  I am burdened with tragic dream.

But when twilight quietly knocks
  At the door of my soul, then O
To fly to the icy rocks,
  And the eternal snow!

## Context Notes

An eight-line lyric in quatrains that counterpoises the valley (balm, calm, but 'tragic dream') with the mountain peaks (icy rocks, eternal snow) as the speaker's desired escape at twilight. The opposition valley/peak structures a large portion of *The Book of Nature*: the cultivated human valley versus the untouched alpine summit. The aspiration 'to fly to the icy rocks, / And the eternal snow!' when 'twilight quietly knocks / At the door of my soul' echoes the Romantic tradition of the mountain sublime as therapy for spiritual anguish: Shelley's 'Mont Blanc,' Byron's *Manfred*. Fletcher's version strips away the philosophical elaboration to a pure cry, eight lines, nothing more.

Form: two quatrains of three-beat lines, abab.
