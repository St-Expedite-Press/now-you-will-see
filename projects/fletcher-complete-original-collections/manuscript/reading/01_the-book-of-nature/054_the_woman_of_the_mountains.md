## Original Lineation

From a height on the sunlit mountains
  I have looked afar,
And I have seen that the ranges
  But one vast woman are.

In nudity tremendous,
  Sleeping as if long dead,
Her body, white as marble,
  Rests on its dark-green bed--

A Titaness left slumbering
  Long since her earthly day;
Her grim, bronze-muscled kinsmen
  Are utterly swept away.

Smooth glaciers form her shining flanks,
  Dark peaks her tangled hair,
And rose-tipped snowy summits
  Are her breasts, which are still and bare.

Sometimes the sky weaves over her
  A robe of cloud in shame,
Down summer and winter drifting
  It goes: she rests the same.

Beneath her superb chasteness
  Forces wait to be called to birth:
Forces of iron and lightning
  That yet shall sway the earth.

For an infinite eternity
  My soul can wait on the peak,
Weaving wild songs of slumber
  Till she wakens: she I seek.

## Context Notes

A Titaness in Greek mythology is a female Titan -- one of the primordial divine race that preceded the Olympians, including Rhea (mother of Zeus), Themis (goddess of law), Mnemosyne (Memory), and others. Fletcher personifies the entire Alpine mountain range as a sleeping Titaness of superhuman scale: 'Smooth glaciers form her shining flanks, / Dark peaks her tangled hair, / And rose-tipped snowy summits / Are her breasts.' The phrase 'Her grim, bronze-muscled kinsmen / Are utterly swept away' alludes to the Titanomachy (the war in which the Olympian gods defeated and imprisoned the Titans), placing the sleeping mountain range as the last survivor of a defeated supernatural order. The poem's seven stanzas alternate between physical description (geological features as body parts) and spiritual apprehension (forces 'that yet shall sway the earth'), ending with the speaker as a solitary watcher waiting for the Titaness to wake -- an image of erotic and creative longing simultaneously.

Form: seven ballad quatrains in three-beat lines, rhymed abcb.
