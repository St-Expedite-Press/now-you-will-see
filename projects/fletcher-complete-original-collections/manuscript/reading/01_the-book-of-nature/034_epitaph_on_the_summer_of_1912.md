## Original Lineation

Strayed far from out her father Sun's domain,
Lifelong she wept; but all her tears were vain.
Her broken-hearted mother Earth now rears
This stone to her, and wets it with her tears.

## Context Notes

A four-line verse epitaph in the classical Latin tradition of lapidary inscription (short poems meant to be carved on stone). The personification of summer as a wanderer who strayed from her father the Sun and died of weeping while Mother Earth erected this stone follows Roman convention (grave monuments frequently recorded that a family member raised the stone and wept). The irony is that the 'stone' is a printed poem: the epitaph form is applied to a season, the memorial medium to an elegy for an English summer's failure. Paired with the preceding 'Death of the Summer, 1912,' the two poems form a brief diptych -- elegy followed by monument.

Form: two heroic couplets -- the lapidary shape the note above describes; four lines fit for a stone.
