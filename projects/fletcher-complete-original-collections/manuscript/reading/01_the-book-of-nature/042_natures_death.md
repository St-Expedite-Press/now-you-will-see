## Original Lineation

I stand where beats the sea
  Tireless, again and again:
'Tis crying tragically
  Its old mysterious pain.
Beat by the shattered spray,
  Stunned by the endless roar,
I watch the storm-clouds grey
  That trail upon the shore.

Black riven crags of rock
  Repel each wave's mad spring:
Great Nature, shock on shock,
  Rages in suffering.
Man dreams to tame or slay
  Her mad unbounded might:
One little hour of day
  Is his, ere falls the night.

Weaving rebellious hymns
  Of hopeless blasphemy,
Great Nature too hath dreams,
  God's power to defy.

## Context Notes

Part III closes with this poem in which the speaker stands at the sea's edge and hears Nature raging -- 'shock on shock, / Rages in suffering' -- in a figure for the cosmic struggle against divine authority ('Great Nature too hath dreams, / God's power to defy'). The poem belongs to the Romantic tradition of the storm-sublime (Byron's *Childe Harold*, Canto IV; Swinburne's 'By the North Sea') where the speaker's own turbulence is externalised into the sea's violence. The closing image of Nature weaving 'rebellious hymns / Of hopeless blasphemy' against God anticipates the persistent counter-religious strain in *Fools' Gold* and *Visions of the Evening*. The title 'Nature's Death' is ironic: what the poem actually depicts is Nature in its most violent life.

Form: two octaves and a closing quatrain in three-beat lines, cross-rhymed abab throughout; the final quatrain goes slant ('hymns/dreams') just as the blasphemy arrives.
