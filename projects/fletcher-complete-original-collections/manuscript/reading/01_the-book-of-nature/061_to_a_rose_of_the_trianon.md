## Original Lineation

Tell me, O exquisite rose that abidest
Here, 'mid the relics of a beauty faded,
From what dust hast thou gathered that proud
  essence
Of perfume and colour that thou giv'st to the
  world?
Thou that hast sucked the heat of the rain-washed
  marbles,
Tremulously flushing with crimson in the sunlight,
Careless thou bloomest, rose of love unaltered,
Though all men's hate and pride about thee fall in
  ruin--
As though all the ages were not--as though the
  world were made new.

## Editorial Relineation

Tell me, O exquisite rose that abidest
Here, 'mid the relics of a beauty faded,
From what dust hast thou gathered that proud essence
Of perfume and colour that thou giv'st to the world?
Thou that hast sucked the heat of the rain-washed marbles,
Tremulously flushing with crimson in the sunlight,
Careless thou bloomest, rose of love unaltered,
Though all men's hate and pride about thee fall in ruin--
As though all the ages were not--as though the world were made new.

## Context Notes

A rose interrogated in a royal French garden has Ronsard behind it -- 'Mignonne, allons voir si la rose' -- but Fletcher inverts the carpe diem. Ronsard's rose is the lesson: gather your youth, the petals fall. Fletcher's rose has outlived the gatherers, blooming 'careless... rose of love unaltered' while 'all men's hate and pride about thee fall in ruin.' The flower does not receive the lesson; it gives one -- the court is dust and the rose is punctual, 'as though the world were made new.'

The Trianon is the pair of lesser palaces in the park of Versailles; the Petit Trianon was Marie Antoinette's retreat. 'The relics of a beauty faded' had a tenant.

Form: nine unrhymed long lines in a falling, faintly sapphic rhythm, the last line stretched past all the rest -- the rose's exemption written into the measure.
