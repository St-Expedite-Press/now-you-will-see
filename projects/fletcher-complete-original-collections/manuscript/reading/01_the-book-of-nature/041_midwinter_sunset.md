## Original Lineation

The clouds blown together, like ragged whorls of
  smoke,
Stretch long and twisted fingers up in the west:
And in their grip hangs weltering, half extin-
  guished,
  The ruby of the sun.

The wind like a cripple rolls over dark purple
  moors;
And in the hollows the old bare beeches sing
A ballad of winter, while in their dry, stirring
  leaves
A frightened squirrel scurries off in dismay.

## Editorial Relineation

The clouds blown together, like ragged whorls of smoke,
Stretch long and twisted fingers up in the west:
And in their grip hangs weltering, half extinguished,
  The ruby of the sun.

The wind like a cripple rolls over dark purple moors;
And in the hollows the old bare beeches sing
A ballad of winter, while in their dry, stirring leaves
A frightened squirrel scurries off in dismay.

## Context Notes

An eight-line poem (two unrhymed quatrains in the editorial lineation) describing a midwinter sunset on the English moors. 'Whorls of smoke' is an accurate description of cumulus clouds when viewed in low angle light. The central image -- 'in their grip hangs weltering, half extinguished, / The ruby of the sun' -- gives the sun as a semi-precious stone grasped by cloud-fingers, a Decadent jewel-metaphor in the manner of Pater and Wilde. 'Purple moors' establishes an English (likely northern) setting -- Dartmoor, Exmoor, or the Yorkshire moors -- with the beeches' 'ballad of winter' completing a very English autumnal scene. 'Weltering' means tossing or heaving turbulently; an archaic word retained for its force.

Form: two unrhymed quatrains in long accentual lines, the first dropping to a short close -- 'The ruby of the sun' -- where the grip tightens.
