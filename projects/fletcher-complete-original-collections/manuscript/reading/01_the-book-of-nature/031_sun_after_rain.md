## Original Lineation

After the long rain
The sun sprang forth again,
The light that conquers pain.

A church-bell far away
Melodiously asway
Threw out a throbbing gay.

Like fountain-spray in air,
The light was shattered where
Before 'twas bleak and bare.

The whole sky shook with light:
The earth quivered with might:
And nowhere was there night.

## Context Notes

Twelve lines in three quatrains of monorhyme (pain / again; day / gay; bare / where; light / might / night), with the third stanza departing into a different set of rhymes as the poem releases into exclamation. The subject -- sun returning after rain -- is one of the oldest lyric occasions; Fletcher's version is stripped of any natural description and works entirely through abstract registers of sensation ('the light that conquers pain,' 'shattered where,' 'quivered with might'). 'The whole sky shook with light' echoes Keats's description of the sun over the sea in *Endymion* (1818), where light is similarly experienced as a physical force. The poem belongs to a tradition of epiphanic lyrics in which sudden meteorological change serves as a figure for psychological or spiritual transformation.

Form: four monorhymed tercets -- 'rain/again/pain' through 'light/might/night' -- not three quatrains, as the note above counts them. The triple rhyme tolls like the church-bell of the second stanza.
