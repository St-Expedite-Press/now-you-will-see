## Original Lineation

It is the thin haze-mist
  Which, near or distant, clings
Upon the bare rough hills,
  Half veiling shapes of things.

It makes the ranges seem
  Like a great sea bronze-cast;
It throws pale lights amid
  Their roller-shadows vast.

Like a thin spray it is,
  As it melts out here and there
To the sunlit windless air,
  In the sky-realms of bliss.

Into those pale sky-realms
  Half hid by grey, that fill
With shadowy light the sea
  That, mystically, is still.

## Context Notes

A meditation on atmospheric haze that uses the haze itself as a structural principle: the poem becomes vaguer and more diffuse as it proceeds, the final stanza dissolving into 'shadowy light' and stillness. 'Roller-shadows' are the moving shadows cast by long wavelike ridges -- an exact visual observation. The simile 'Like a great sea bronze-cast' reappears across Fletcher's Italian and Swiss poems: the Alps seen as a petrified sea, a frozen sublime. The final turn -- 'Into those pale sky-realms / Half hid by grey, that fill / With shadowy light the sea / That, mystically, is still' -- echoes the aesthetics of Turner's late paintings, where atmosphere and subject become nearly indistinguishable. Fletcher was familiar with the visual arts tradition.

Form: four quatrains of three-beat lines, rhymed abcb except the third, which closes to an envelope (abba) and then lets go -- the scheme losing and finding its edges like the haze it describes.
