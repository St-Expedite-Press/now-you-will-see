## Original Lineation

The long thin swaying curtains of the rain
Are tangled in the bare boughs of the plain.

Down-sloping from the sky, grey-mottled o'er,
Their fringes trail the sodden valley floor.

Heavy with woe, they dusk the day's grey light;
Heavy, they stir and rustle all the night:

And the steep crumbling bridge that leads to town
Quivers beneath the torrent rushing down.

Some mystery is now shaping 'neath these veils;
Which none may see, till, rent by northern gales,

They suddenly fly apart: then, 'yond them, lo!
The high, untrodden peaks of sparkling snow!

## Context Notes

This poem opens 'Part I: The Months in Italy,' a twelve-poem sequence running from January through December that frames the whole of *The Book of Nature*. Fletcher's January is set in an Italian valley — the low plains visible from an upland, the 'steep crumbling bridge' over a winter torrent — and the drama is purely meteorological: curtains of rain obscure the peaks until northern gales rend them apart to reveal 'the high, untrodden peaks of sparkling snow.' Six iambic-pentameter couplets, each printed as a stanza apart, plain for a poem whose function is threshold.
