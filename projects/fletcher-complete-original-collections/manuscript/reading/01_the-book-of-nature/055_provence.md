## Original Lineation

Blanched castles crumbling on dark-red rocks:
  Desolation under bronze skies:
  Fever's mad monotonies:
Wine of life that burns the throat.

Long ago there were fountains here,
  Shivering and shuddering with dark-green
  waves.
  And colonnades, and cool arcades,
And shadowy lakes in the long ago.

But the sun has baked the roof,
  And the wind has eaten thereof:
  Time has undone the woof,
And Death has danced on love!

Now all is but a monotonous song
  Of memory and ennui,
  A dry and dancing melody
Which the wind whistles all day long.

## Context Notes

Provence is the ancient Roman province (*Provincia Romana*) in southeastern France -- the region around Aix-en-Provence, Arles, and the Rhône delta -- famous for its troubadour poetry tradition (the Occitan lyric of the twelfth and thirteenth centuries), its ruined Roman monuments, and its landscape of lavender, scrubland, and sharp light. Fletcher's Provence is one of ruin and desiccation: 'Blanched castles crumbling on dark-red rocks,' 'Fever's mad monotonies.' The reference to 'colonnades' and 'cool arcades' in the past tense recalls the Roman infrastructure of the province (the Pont du Gard aqueduct, the amphitheatre at Arles); 'Time has undone the woof, / And Death has danced on love' echoes Swinburne's 'The Triumph of Time' and other Swinburnean elegies for destroyed beauty. 'Ennui' closes the poem as the French Symbolists close so many of theirs.

Form: four quatrains, each rhymed to its own rule -- the first leaves its outer lines unrhymed, the second rhymes inside the line ('colonnades, and cool arcades'), the third crosses ('roof/woof,' 'thereof/love'), the fourth encloses. A scheme of monotonies that never once repeats itself.
