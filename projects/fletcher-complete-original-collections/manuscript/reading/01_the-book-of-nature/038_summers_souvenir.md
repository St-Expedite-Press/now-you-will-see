## Original Lineation

The winter wind
  Wearily sighs
Through heavy oak boughs,
  Where the dead leaf dies.

Across the dull sky
  The dark clouds go;
Like withered witches
  They brew the snow.

On the walls of my heart
  Hangs summer's face:
An old portrait
  With a shy, wild grace.

It is curtain-covered
  And hidden away,
And I watch it moulder
  Day after day.

## Context Notes

A brief lyric in which 'summer's face' is preserved as an 'old portrait / With a shy, wild grace' hanging on the walls of the speaker's heart -- 'curtain-covered / And hidden away.' The poem adapts the Romantic and Pre-Raphaelite tradition of the portrait-poem (Rossetti's *The House of Life* sonnet sequences organized around paintings and faces) to the interior landscape of memory. The winter wind 'wearily sighs / Through heavy oak boughs' and 'Like withered witches / They brew the snow' -- the comparison of dark winter clouds to old women ('witches') preparing something sinister is a folk-imagery commonplace in English and German Romantic poetry. The steady quatrains (trimeter, ABCB) keep the poem compact and deliberately simple, like a folk song.

Form: four quatrains of two-beat lines, rhymed abcb.
