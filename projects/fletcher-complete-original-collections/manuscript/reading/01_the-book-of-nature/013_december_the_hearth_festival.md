## Original Lineation

There are midwinter sunsets, fierce and red,
And there are early twilights and long nights,
And there is goodly talk about the fire.

There is endless rain blown up and down the vales,
There are storms and torrents ravening on the hills,
And there is goodly talk about the fire.

There are the kettles singing on the sticks,
There are roast chestnuts and the ancient wine,
And there is goodly talk about the fire.

## Context Notes

The poem answers winter the way Horace answers it in Odes I.9 ('Vides ut alta stet nive candidum / Soracte'): pile wood on the fire, open the wine. Fletcher makes the same answer three times over — storm and torrent get two lines each, and every stanza closes on 'goodly talk about the fire.' Three tercets of unrhymed pentameter, each sealed by the refrain; weather in the open lines, fire always in the last.
