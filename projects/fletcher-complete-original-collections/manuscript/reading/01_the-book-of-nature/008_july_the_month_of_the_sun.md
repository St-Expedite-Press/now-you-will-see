## Original Lineation

Within the steep and steel-blue dome of sky
  There is no stir nor murmur, swift or soft:
  The sun bears steadily his torch aloft,
Striding up to the zenith, where on high
  The sleepless God whose worshipper he is
Watches another day flash past in flame:
A day whose glory, worship, perfect fame,
  Are but to breathe in an immobile bliss.

Now bees and brutes and men leave drink and
    food;
Sunk in a mighty reverie the earth,
Teeming with passionate joy, perfected pain,
Wings on its tireless path so oft pursued,
Through day and night, through death and life
    and birth,
Through suns supreme and thunder-showers of
    rain.

## Editorial Relineation

Within the steep and steel-blue dome of sky
  There is no stir nor murmur, swift or soft:
  The sun bears steadily his torch aloft,
Striding up to the zenith, where on high

  The sleepless God whose worshipper he is
Watches another day flash past in flame:
A day whose glory, worship, perfect fame,
  Are but to breathe in an immobile bliss.

Now bees and brutes and men leave drink and food;
Sunk in a mighty reverie the earth,
Teeming with passionate joy, perfected pain,
Wings on its tireless path so oft pursued,

Through day and night, through death and life and birth,
Through suns supreme and thunder-showers of rain.

## Context Notes

A sonnet structured as a vertical ascent and a return: the octave moves upward through sky, sun, zenith, and ‘the sleepless God,’ then the sestet turns at l. 9 (‘Now’) and descends to bees, brutes, men, and the earth’s noon-stilled reverie. The poem’s central paradox is in that turn — beneath absolute immobility, the world continues ‘through day and night, through death and life and birth.’ The source print has line wraps throughout; the editorial relineation restores the full metrical lines, dividing the sonnet 4+4+4+2.
