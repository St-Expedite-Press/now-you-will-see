## Original Lineation

The pale sun strikes a last sad glimmer of gold
From the worn and decrepit sides of crumbling
  palaces,
Flaunting their shameless pride of slatternliness,
Frowning in monomaniac obsessions of the past.
Yet even after their walls have drunken the latest
  sorrows,
Have swallowed the final glow of impurity, poverty,
  lust,
And relapsed into the dumb melancholy of senility,
A brass laugh rings in the silence:--
There is a gleam of eyes, a rustle of skirts, a flirt
  of fans.
After us, let there come exhaustion, madness, and
  death.

## Context Notes

A prose poem in all but name: a single paragraph in free verse whose governing image is the last light of day on crumbling palazzo facades. 'Slatternliness' (slovenly dirtiness, disorderly neglect) applied to decaying palaces is another willful deflation of architectural grandeur, in the manner of Browning and Ruskin's descriptions of Rome and Venice after the fall of their greatness. The poem's closing gesture -- 'There is a gleam of eyes, a rustle of skirts, a flirt of fans. / After us, let there come exhaustion, madness, and death' -- transforms the Decadent scene into a moment of ephemeral pleasure in the manner of Verlaine's *Fêtes galantes* (1869), those impressionistic masquerade-garden poems in which masked figures make love before an uncertain darkness.

Form: free verse, a single ten-line strophe; the last two lines break from description into speech.
