## Original Lineation

To my soul which is as drunken
  With summer's anguish of silent leaves,
Dead infantas go trailing by,
  And the swallow is one that grieves
For a past, which the trees have not forgotten,
For they suck in its strength with their leaves dead
  and rotten.

O the long, long silent sadness
  Of summer which weeps not for evermore!
Like a boat adrift on the dark sea's gladness
  The sunlight seeks in vain a shore:
And back through the thin dry sky it must pass,
From the drooping trees and the withered grass.

Brood in unechoing branches, woe:
  But let no words mark what must be.
Wait till the fading yet darkening glow
  Of the sun gone down in the west you see:
Then in tearless sobs let your grief pass away
In the arms of old twilight, deaf and grey.

## Context Notes

Set in the Versailles gardens, the poem uses 'dead infantas' -- the Spanish-Portuguese word *infanta* means princess of royal blood, here presumably the shades of Versailles's royal ladies who walked these same paths -- to give the summer sadness a historical spectral dimension. The poem is structured in three stanzas of six lines each, with an unusual rhyme scheme that changes stanza by stanza, enacting the 'sad waltz' of the first line. 'Drooping trees' and 'withered grass' are the gardens in late summer beginning to turn. The closing apostrophe -- 'Wait till the fading yet darkening glow / Of the sun gone down in the west you see: / Then in tearless sobs let your grief pass away / In the arms of old twilight, deaf and grey' -- gives sunset as the only available grief-ritual: not weeping but surrendering to the failing light.

Form: three sixains in long swaying lines, the last two rhymed ababcc, the first looser, its couplet 'forgotten/rotten' the only firm footing. One caution on the note above: there is no 'sad waltz' in this poem; the phrase quoted there is not Fletcher's.
