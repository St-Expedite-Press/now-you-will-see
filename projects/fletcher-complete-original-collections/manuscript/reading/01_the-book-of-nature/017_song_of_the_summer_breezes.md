## Original Lineation

We dance with pulsating feet
Through the wild wilderness.
  We, the sons of delight,
Who know not any sorrow,
Who take no thought of the morrow,
  Indifferent to day or night,
Warmed by the sun's caress,
Finding existence sweet.

We make our bacchanals
  Wherever the dark grape is swaying
  Heavy with juice, on the vine,
Or the shepherd's flute-note calls.
The sunlight, the joy of the ocean,
Weaves itself with our motion:
  But the nymphs of the rocks, half divine,
Detain us, intoxicated.
--And we sleep until day is belated.

## Context Notes

The opening octave rhymes abcddcba (feet / wilderness / delight / sorrow / morrow / night / caress / sweet) — an enclosed scheme that contradicts the poem's subject: breezes 'who know not any sorrow' are bound inside a circular return. The 'nymphs of the rocks, half divine' (l. 26) are Oreads, mountain nymphs of Greek myth, who detain the breezes and put them to sleep — the Homeric pattern of divine figures who delay wanderers (Calypso, Circe). An envelope octave followed by a nine-line strophe rhymed abca ddcee, printed solid in the source.
