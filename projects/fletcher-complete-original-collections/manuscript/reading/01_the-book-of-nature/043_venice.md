## Original Lineation

I have seen Venice old and grey with years
  Losing her faded gewgaws one by one.
Nothing more sad than this to me appears
  Betwixt the morning and the evening sun.

It seemed to me my heart enrobed with tears
  Was ceasing to long for all things under the
  sun:
When nothing now the heart of Venice hears
  But one great stir and frenzy to have done!

If all the things we once so loved in life
  Seem now but trash to be swept into the sea:
Let Death, that pallid ennui, free from strife,
  In taking Venice not pass over me!

O life of commonplace vast joys and tears,
  O city whose proud dream of life is done,
Let the snow fall like ashes through the years,
  For these have vanished, leaving me alone.

## Context Notes

Part IV of *The Book of Nature* is titled 'Italy--Bavaria--Switzerland--Provence--America,' and Venice opens it. Venice in the Edwardian imagination was the preeminent city of beautiful decay, celebrated by Ruskin (*The Stones of Venice*, 1851-53), Browning ('A Toccata of Galuppi's'), Henry James, and countless others as a figure for gorgeous decline. 'Gewgaws' (trinkets, cheap ornaments) applied to Venice's architectural splendors is a willfully deflationary word, aligning Fletcher with the Decadent tradition that sees Venice's grandeur as show covering death. The poem is a sonnet, though irregular: the final stanza has six lines, making it a sestet rather than a standard Petrarchan octave-sestet division. The addressal 'O city' at the opening of the final sestet is a Shelleyan or Swinburnean apostrophe. 'Ennui' (French: boredom, spiritual emptiness, existential tedium) is one of Fletcher's key Baudelairean terms.

Form: four pentameter quatrains, abab -- sixteen lines, not the sonnet described above. Three of the four stanzas turn on the same rhyme-pair ('years/sun' and its kin): the poem keeps re-rhyming itself, an exhaustion built into the scheme and proper to the city.
