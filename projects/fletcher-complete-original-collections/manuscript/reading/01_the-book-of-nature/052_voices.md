## Original Lineation

The voices of the grass are harsh and shrill:
Cold and flute-like are the voices on the hill.
A bodiless sighing is the voice amid the boughs:
Clamorous are the voices where the torrent flows.
A tapping and a clapping are the voices of the
  creek:
And an empty silence is the voice upon the peak.

## Editorial Relineation

The voices of the grass are harsh and shrill:
Cold and flute-like are the voices on the hill.
A bodiless sighing is the voice amid the boughs:
Clamorous are the voices where the torrent flows.
A tapping and a clapping are the voices of the creek:
And an empty silence is the voice upon the peak.

## Context Notes

A six-line catalogue poem in which each line names a landscape feature and characterises its voice. The form is an ancient one -- the catalogue of natural attributes -- found in Psalms, classical catalogues of rivers and winds, and the Romantic loco-descriptive tradition. Fletcher's version is compressed to a single sentence in parallel clauses, ending with the paradox 'an empty silence is the voice upon the peak': the summit, reached at last, says nothing. This anti-climactic silence is the recurring destination of many poems in *The Book of Nature*, and the editorial relineation corrects the typeset line-breaks to show that the poem is six long lines, not the hyphenated fragments of the original printing.

Form: six long lines in three couplets, one voice to a line; the closing couplet, where a couplet should clinch loudest, ends on 'an empty silence.'
