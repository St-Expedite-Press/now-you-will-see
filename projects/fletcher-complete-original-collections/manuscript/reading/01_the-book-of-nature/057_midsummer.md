## Original Lineation

The waves shake the long wet beach,
  Crashing in thunderous monotone:
The dazzling sails far out of reach
  Are tossed about and blown.

The sun, like a million hammer-falls,
  Bangs on the anvil of my brain:
Daggers of pain at the eyeballs
  Stab, again and again:

And all is brutal joy, despite
  The poison and sweat on which I sup
When the black sword of the night
  Shears off the sun, and the blood spouts up.

## Context Notes

A three-stanza lyric whose imagery of violent summer intensity ('The sun, like a million hammer-falls, / Bangs on the anvil of my brain') draws on the industrial-sublime that Fletcher was simultaneously developing in *The Dominant City*. The collocation of solar power and industrial force (hammer, anvil) places summer's heat in the same register as city machinery. The closing image -- 'when the black sword of the night / Shears off the sun, and the blood spouts up' -- is deliberately violent and synaesthetic: sunset as decapitation, the sky's red aftermath as blood. The poem belongs to the tradition of the midsummer lyric as a poem of painful excess rather than pastoral ease; compare Keats's 'To Autumn,' where the season is ripe to bursting, or Tennyson's 'Maud' where summer heat correlates with feverish erotic passion.

Form: three quatrains rhymed abab, iambic-anapestic four-beat lines.
