## Original Lineation

Full moon, and night's great hush
On grass and flower and bush:
The motionless calm of the trees
Standing in shadowy peace
Down the long road, far away
To the white borders of day.

Through a sparkling ocean of air
The moonlight is scattered, where
There is only smiling sleep,
And the stars that move on the deep.
But the flowers then, men say,
Meditate on the day.

## Context Notes

A twelve-line lyric whose central conceit -- that flowers 'meditate on the day' during the moonlit night -- derives from the occult-botanical tradition (the 'language of flowers' and plant sensitivity to light, popularized in the nineteenth century) and from Romantic moon-poetry. The line 'the stars that move on the deep' treats the night sky as an ocean, a synaesthetic reversal common in French Symbolism (Mallarmé, Verlaine) and carried into English poetry by the late Decadents. The poem's two equal hexastichs (six-line stanzas) frame the movement from plain description (moonlit road, trees) to metaphysical observation (flowers' meditation), and the final couplet's easy assurance -- 'But the flowers then, men say' -- signals the folk-knowledge register Fletcher sometimes adopts.

Form: two sixains in three-beat couplets, aabbcc.
