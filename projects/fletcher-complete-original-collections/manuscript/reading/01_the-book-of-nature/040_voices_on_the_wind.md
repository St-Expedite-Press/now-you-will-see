## Original Lineation

Wind and the whirling of white mists,
  Through the desolate night.
And there are voices on the wind:
Roaring horns, hoarse cries of battle!
  The dark wind,
Endlessly whirling the white mists.

  The pale mists
Wavering, unsteady, break before dawn:
  They hurriedly vanish.
The low talk, the sombre yells of despair,
These are lost on the long wind:
But the jarring thunder of sea-chariots racing
Shoreward, yet holds my soul and keeps it from
  gaining sleep.

## Context Notes

A short free-verse lyric in which the speaker hears battle-sounds on the wind at night -- 'Roaring horns, hoarse cries of battle!' -- and cannot sleep. The 'sea-chariots' of the final line are an unusual compound: the image recalls descriptions of Norse or Homeric sea-raiding, where ships are called 'horses of the sea' or 'sea-steeds.' The poem formally uses a frame repetition (the whirling mists opening and closing the first section, the pale mists dispersing in the second) that gives it the feeling of a ballad refrain without using one literally. The nocturnal battle-sounds heard from a distance recall the tradition of the watch poem or *alba* (dawn song), but inverted: here the sounds of strife prevent sleep rather than signal time to leave.

Form: free verse in two strophes, six lines and seven, the first ringed by its own mists ('whirling the white mists' opening and closing it); the final line runs long past every other and withholds sleep as the syntax withholds its verb.
