## Original Lineation

Night, and wilderness everywhere!
A wilderness that shares my care:
For the wind in the weary pine tree wakes,
In its feather bed it tosses and shakes.
One star like a taper at the head
Gleams low, unflickeringly, and red:
Death watches the patient, half-way dead.

As for me, passion has drained me dry:
Black, bitter dregs in my heart now lie,
And the awful thirst of fierce despair
I cannot shake off anywhere!
'O let dawn come or let me die!'
I groan: and is there no reply?

A cloud rolls over the single star:
Blackness--void--silence--near and far....

## Context Notes

A twelve-line lyric that uses the wilderness night as an externalisation of spiritual desolation. 'One star like a taper at the head / Gleams low, unflickeringly, and red: / Death watches the patient, half-way dead' -- the star as a candle held at the head of a deathbed, and Death as the attending physician or nurse, are images from the tradition of the *ars moriendi* (art of dying) meditative literature. The poem's final movement -- 'A cloud rolls over the single star: / Blackness--void--silence--near and far' -- ends with three nouns in a dash-separated list, each as absolute as death, which formally enacts the collapse the poem describes. The wilderness setting links this poem to the American frontier literature Fletcher grew up with in Arkansas, as well as to the biblical wilderness tradition.

Form: fifteen lines, not the twelve counted above -- a seven-line strophe in couplets sealed by a triplet ('head/red/dead': taper, glow, watcher), a sixain rhymed aabbaa, and a last couplet snuffed out with the star.
