Stepping cautiously across the threshold from Highway 65 into Mount Holly Cemetery, I knew they would still be there. Roses...the Stephen F. Austin roses bloomed everywhere; grass erupted from the ground; the sky was sapphire blue and cloudless. *They're still there.* Sure enough, there they were: a small line of corroding coins, Jefferson face up and Lincoln face down, along the edge of John Gould Fletcher's gravesite. But who was he?

"This juvenile outpouring," Fletcher had once inscribed to E. McKnight Kauffer, the avant-garde illustrator, "of the moderately good and the atrociously bad," was a characteristic expression of the author himself. Fletcher produced five poetry books in the year 1913, of which *Fool's Gold*, the one in which the inscription sits, was likely the fourth. One of them is his first published work, although there is some confusion as to which.

Those five books—*The Book of Nature*, *The Dominant City*, *Fire and Wine*, *Fool's Gold*, and *Visions of the Evening*—belong to the strange, unsettled opening of Fletcher's career, and to the larger disorder out of which American modernism and *Southern* modernism began speaking. Together, they show the young Fletcher at the dangerous moment before his own certainty: imitative, excessive, often embarrassing, sometimes startlingly original, already moving toward the chromatic, synesthetic, and architectural ambitions that would make him one of the odder and more consequential American modernists. Until now, they have never been committed together.

But again, *who was John Gould Fletcher?* This is not a biography.

John Gould Fletcher was born in Little Rock, Arkansas, in 1886, into a prominent Southern family whose inheritance he never quite escaped. He studied at Harvard, left upon his father's death without taking a degree, and spent much of his early adult life abroad, especially in England.

The fact of England should not be allowed to overmaster the fact of Arkansas. The early poems move through Italian months, northern abstractions, antique groves, mythic processions, and the exhausted décor of Europe; yet beneath their borrowed marbles and foreign skies one already feels the pressure of origin. Fletcher's imagination is not simply cosmopolitan. It is fugitive. It carries place by flight from place, and the very extravagance of its landscapes betrays the unease of inheritance.

After failing to place his poems conventionally, Fletcher arranged to finance the volumes himself, distributing them across several publishers---Constable for *The Book of Nature*, Max Goschen for *The Dominant City* and *Fool's Gold*, Grant Richards for *Fire and Wine*, and Erskine Macdonald for *Visions of the Evening*. They appeared together in May 1913, less as a debut than as a forced entrance: a young poet buying his way into print before any school, movement, or audience had quite made room for him.

Their simultaneity is part of their meaning. These are not five isolated juvenilia but one overburdened act of self-declaration: a whole weather of first attempts, failures, imitations, discoveries, and premonitions. The young Fletcher appears not as a settled poet but as an atmosphere gathering force, uncertain of its forms and yet already governed by the need to make color, sound, season, and architecture answer to one another.

His career after that was restless, often brilliant, and tremendously uneven. He wrote poetry, criticism, autobiography, and regional reflection, moving between Europe, the American literary world, and Arkansas, always pulled between cosmopolitan experiment and the burden of place. In 1939, he became the first Southern poet to win the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry, for *Selected Poems*. His later years were marked by illness, isolation, and recurring depression; in 1950, he drowned himself in a pond near his home in Little Rock. What remains is a strange and oddly ignored body of work: early decorative excess, imagist experiment, Southern memory, and the effort of a poet trying to make color, landscape, and consciousness speak in the same breath.

The present edition does not seek to correct that strangeness into polish. Its wager is more modest and more severe: that Fletcher's early excess should be seen clearly, not rescued from itself, not apologized away, and certainly not buried under the later reputation. The poems must be allowed their awkward splendor, their derivative gestures, their sudden felicities, their strange anticipations of the later chromatic and regional imagination.

Fletcher's early books were printed by a scattershot of boutique houses including T. and A. Constable, Printers to His Majesty, at the Edinburgh University Press, and their pages often flatten effects that appear to belong to the poems' own visual and musical logic. In some cases this flattening likely reflects contemporary convention; in others, ordinary technical constraints of page width, type, indentation, and line carry-over.

My editorial impositions in this edition are usually not interpretive rewritings, but acts of recovery at the level of presentation. Where I have intervened, it is generally to typeset, re-lineate, or re-indent the poems so that their stanzaic movement, staggered emphasis, and rhythmic architecture can be read without the accidents of the original printing passing silently as poetic intention. More intrusive, perhaps, are the moments when I provide context that would not have been necessary for a literate London reader of the early 20th century.

My editorial voice is present here, but out of respect for the work itself, I will reserve fullest commentary for the afterword.

Here, I wish only to assemble the story and allow John Gould Fletcher to speak for himself.
