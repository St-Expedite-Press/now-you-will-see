## Original Lineation

See my hands bleed with toiling, and my eyes
Are weary with the sun upon the grain;
My body is naught but one great ache of pain:
Taskmaster, blind and idle, grant the prize,
Sleep that descends and never lifts again!

Unripe and thin are many of my sheaves:
Some are unbound and scattered wide, and some
The rats have gnawed: this is the harvest home;
But not for bygone folly my heart grieves.

The high and hollow winds brought down the
        frost:
Mayhap I dallied: idleness was bliss:
Or I was weak: at any rate, I lost:
Yet do I not repent me, even of this.

Let laughing gleaners find the ripest ears:
For me the sun is out, nor night holds care;
The open sky is full of steely stars,
I sup with death, and wrap me in despair.

## Context Notes

Four stanzas in a modified Petrarchan sonnet form (ABBA ABBA CDC DCD -- actually 16 lines spread across four quatrains, with an editorial relineation), the 'Conclusion' of *Fools' Gold* completes Part III: The Failure with an extended agricultural metaphor of the failed harvest. The imagery draws on the Parable of the Sower (Matthew 13:1-23), where some grain falls on good soil and some is lost, and on the tradition of the harvest as life-review: 'Unripe and thin are many of my sheaves: / Some are unbound and scattered wide, and some / The rats have gnawed: this is the harvest home.' 'Harvest home' is both the celebration at the end of harvest (a traditional English folk festival) and, ironically, the name for what is being gathered here -- a failed crop. The speaker does not repent: 'Yet do I not repent me, even of this.' The final image -- 'I sup with death, and wrap me in despair' -- is a Swiftian last supper with mortality, and the phrase 'steely stars' gives the open sky a cold, indifferent beauty consistent with the collection's persistent nihilism.
