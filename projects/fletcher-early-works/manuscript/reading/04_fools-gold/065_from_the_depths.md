## Original Lineation

Because it is ever night,
Because the fire is out,
Because I have fought my fight,
There rises the last doubt.

Because the crimson glow
Of my volcanic soul
Has only blazed to show
The fogs that round me roll:

Because I am nailed to my cross
Alone, nor hope to break
This dreary tale of loss;
Nor my sky-city make:

Because I am astray,
An exile from all lands
Set free to do and say
Nothing, I drop my hands;

I bow my head; the night
May take me if it will:
Longer I cannot fight.
At last my soul is still.

But yet, you mob abhorred
Of sty-engendered wits,
You shall not have my sword:
I stamp its blade to bits.

## Context Notes

Six ballad quatrains in ABAB form, the poem is built on anaphoric 'Because' clauses (a structure used in Psalms of lament -- 'because the enemy hath persecuted my soul' -- and in formal supplication) that accumulate justifications for surrender before the final defiant reversal. 'I am nailed to my cross / Alone' is a deliberate Christological image: crucifixion as metaphor for isolated suffering appears repeatedly in the collection, but here it is stripped of redemptive meaning -- there is no resurrection, only 'this dreary tale of loss.' The poem's turn in the final quatrain -- refusing to give the 'mob abhorred' the sword, instead stamping it to bits -- is a characteristic gesture of refusing to be defeated by one's enemies while also refusing continued struggle: the sword is neither surrendered nor used. 'Sty-engendered wits' (a sty being a pigsty) figures the contemptible mob as livestock, with their intellects bred in filth.
