## Original Lineation

To the leaf on the ground
Spake the leaf on the tree:--
Every summer I try
The more lovely to be:
'Tis a fatal illusion,
Which has the support
Of a million mad beings
Who think life is sport.
What myriads of leaves
Were born and have died
To fill but one tree:
To count have you tried?
Had there been but one leaf
In the wide world entire,
I might know no more grief,
Feel no more desire!
To a bright imperfection
Like thine I did grow:
Then naught else but decay
Could I do: so I go
To that peace where at peace
With all growth I shall be.
May the wind cast me soon
To the ground from the tree!

Said the leaf on the ground
To the leaf on the tree:--
Dost thou dream me more happy,
More perfect than thee?
Know that, whenever born
In this round-circling hell,
'Tis in vain to escape:
Here yet thou must dwell.
Behold my bright bliss!
By each breeze I am whirled;
I am trod underfoot;
In a heap I am hurled.
There rolling, I change:
And in earth shall you see
Me yet become sap,
And again mount the tree!
O agony vain!
Would now I could fly
Where matter is not,
In a black void on high!
There beyond death and life
To rest, nor to be
Any more upon earth,
A leaf of a tree!

## Context Notes

Two movements of sixteen lines each (ll. 11–34, 36–59), each spoken by one leaf in the dialogue, in ballad-length quatrains (ABCB). The first speaker (leaf on tree) and second speaker (leaf on ground) are headed by prose-style quotation frames in the source.
