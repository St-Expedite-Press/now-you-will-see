## Original Lineation

The lamp of Parliament is like an eye
Which only sees at times, and then at night.
It lends its beams, a strange and tragic sight,
To silly bats and fuddled owls that fly,
Seeking the unreal through the fog's black blight,
Where stars are none nor tubes to see them by.
And so this lamp may plume itself on high;
With pompous pride and yellow lust made bright.

Bale-fire of England flaunting her black years,
Your reign is over: even slaves can see
The foreign shame, the misery, the tears
You make at home: extinguished shall you be,
And fade before the sacred fire of song:
For I am waking, I that slept so long.

## Context Notes

Fourteen lines rhyming ABABBAABCDCDEE — a modified Petrarchan sonnet. The lamp in the poem is the real lamp on the Clock Tower of the Palace of Westminster (now Elizabeth Tower), which burns when Parliament is in session. l. 20 ('Bale-fire'): archaic — a large signal-fire or beacon; from Old English *bǣlfȳr*.
