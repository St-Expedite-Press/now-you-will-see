## Original Lineation

I often think that poets never have sung
Beauty but with a dull and stammering tongue:
That only in dim wonderlands of sleep
To which the key is lost, in drowsing deep,
Is art transformed from out a broken cry
To an immortal, effortless ecstasy.

## Context Notes

Six lines in a single sestet (AABBCC), presenting a compressed version of the argument that the ideal poem exists only in dream: 'only in dim wonderlands of sleep / To which the key is lost, in drowsing deep, / Is art transformed from out a broken cry / To an immortal, effortless ecstasy.' The 'key is lost' is precise: the dreamland of true poetry is not just inaccessible but the means of access has been permanently mislaid, making the gap between waking effort ('dull and stammering tongue') and unconscious perfection absolute. 'Dim wonderlands' may carry a faint echo of Lewis Carroll's *Alice in Wonderland* (1865), though the register is entirely serious. The poem functions as a minimal statement of what the larger poems in this cluster (040-051) elaborate at length.
