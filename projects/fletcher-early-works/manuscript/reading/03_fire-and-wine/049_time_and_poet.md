## Original Lineation

Onward I move, at one with flying Time,
  Borne like a bubble down the resistless stream
Changing yet constant, like a poet's rhyme,
  Containing all, reality and dream.

No more than hours, days, seasons, do I wait:
I watch the terrible spectacle of fate,
  The suns, like kindled matches, flash and fall.
And if too soon I find the hour grown late,
Too soon Time moves without me, hesitate
  Or pause, I cannot: I am part of all.

For if the bubble soon is burst, the stream
  Flies on with newer bubbles, endless Time!
And Time's am I: reality and dream
  Move me to silence or an idle rhyme.

## Context Notes

Three stanzas -- a quatrain (ABAB), a sestet, and a closing quatrain -- meditating on the poet's identity with Time through the bubble metaphor: the individual poet is 'a bubble down the resistless stream,' briefly distinct but ultimately reabsorbed. The image of 'suns, like kindled matches, flash and fall' is characteristic of Fletcher's cosmic scale: the life-spans of suns are measured against the momentary flare of a struck match, making the universe's largest objects as ephemeral as domestic objects. 'Kindled matches' in the early twentieth century would be understood as safety matches, which became widely available in the 1850s; their association with the brief, bright, easily-extinguished flame made them a ready metaphor for mortal life. The poem's resolution -- 'I am part of all' and 'Time's am I' -- echoes Tennyson's *In Memoriam* section XCV and the pantheist endings of earlier poems in the collection, though here the dissolution of individual identity is accepted rather than mourned.
