## Original Lineation

Through day and night and on through many days
  Ever I seek sensations rude and strong.
Like leaves or winds or waves or clouds or rays
  My hosts of song stream endlessly along.

Beyond the keenest joys, beyond the realms of air,
Beyond infinite peace where ended is all care,
Beyond the dark abyss of the most vile despair,
  My hosts of song stream endlessly along.

Sometimes they are a deep and dark-blue pool,
Sometimes a torrent harsh and terrible,
Sometimes a swamp of ennui cold and dull;
But each with all my heart and soul is full.
The infinite only is my bound and rule:
  Stream endlessly along, you hosts of song!

## Context Notes

A fifteen-line lyric in three irregular stanzas, building toward the imperative 'Stream endlessly along, you hosts of song!' The governing metaphor is military: 'hosts' in early twentieth-century usage still carried the primary sense of an armed multitude, a vast army -- Fletcher's songs are conceived as an inexhaustible invading force. The second stanza's anaphoric triple 'Beyond the keenest joys... Beyond infinite peace... Beyond the dark abyss' extends to extremes in both directions: the hosts of song stream beyond joy and beyond despair alike, making the poetic impulse something that transcends emotional contingency. The final stanza's three similes -- 'dark-blue pool,' 'torrent harsh and terrible,' 'swamp of ennui cold and dull' -- cover the full tonal range from lyric melancholy to violent energy to Baudelairean ennui; 'ennui' (a French loanword meaning profound boredom or existential weariness) was a central concept for Baudelaire and the Symbolists.
