## Original Lineation

No speck of mine can ever pass from earth;
  When I am dead I shall relive my pain
  More violently, more exultantly. Again
I suffer, die. Again I pass through birth.

Even my lightest dust about earth blown,
  Men shall consume with eager, greedy lips;
  From clown to monarch, knave to saint, it slips;
All then my own and nothing then my own.

And when the earth at last is dead, and night's
  Brusque curtain cuts in two my latest scene,
  By some collision with a world unseen,
I shall awake before the old footlights.

Ask you perchance for joy's last ecstasy,
  Seek you apart for sorrow's bitter wine,
Would you at one with good or evil be?
  I ask naught, seek naught, need naught. All is
    mine.

## Context Notes

Four stanzas -- three Petrarchan quatrains and a closing irregular sestet -- meditating on the poet's persistence after death through the dissolution and dispersal of his physical substance. The argument is materialist-pantheist: the poet's 'dust' will be inhaled or consumed by all humanity ('from clown to monarch, knave to saint, it slips'), making his being simultaneously universal and self-annihilating. The theatrical conceit of the third stanza -- 'night's / Brusque curtain cuts in two my latest scene' and 'I shall awake before the old footlights' -- figures cosmic death and rebirth as a theatrical performance: the poet as actor who always returns for the next run. 'Old footlights' (the lamps set along the front edge of a stage) gives the cosmic rebirth a mundane theatrical setting. The closing sestet is formally unusual: its final line 'I ask naught, seek naught, need naught. All is / mine.' has the word 'mine' dropped onto a short final line, a typographical enactment of the totalising claim.
