## Original Lineation

I do not condescend to you:
  'Tis to myself I condescend,
  For you are not. You have no end
Nor being, but what I endue--

'Tis this, this clay doll of a day,
  My breath has huddled together so;
  This me, which you can see and know,
That basely would its Father slay.

For Godhood only knows the void,
  Yet yearns for life, and finding it,
  Life finds that Godhood does not fit,
Being precious, with a stuff alloyed;

So all my moments are a crime,
  And all are as idle as all breath,
Which lasts through endless empty time,
  Seeking an empty song of death.

## Context Notes

Four ABBA quatrains in a tight iambic tetrameter, the poem presents a paradox: the poet does not condescend to the public because the public has no independent existence -- 'you are not. You have no end / Nor being, but what I endue.' The speaker's condescension is directed inward, toward the social self he has constructed ('this clay doll of a day') which the public can see. The second stanza's 'clay doll' alludes to the Golem tradition (a figure fashioned from clay and given artificial life) but here the potter is the poet's own breath. The third stanza offers a theodicy of artistic frustration: Godhood 'yearns for life, and finding it, / Life finds that Godhood does not fit, / Being precious, with a stuff alloyed' -- the divine creative impulse cannot survive intact in the material world. The tone and argument recall Nietzsche's division between the artist-creator (who makes the world) and the philistine public (who merely inhabits it).
