## Original Lineation

What is your need, poet,
  What is your need?
"To grow many songs
  From a little thought-seed:
Blossoming myriads,
  Flower and weed."

You are mad indeed, poet,
  You are mad indeed:
When all these have blossomed,
  What is your meed?
Drink, lust, starvation,
  By these you are freed.

## Context Notes

Twelve lines in alternating interrogative and responsive voices -- a question-and-answer structure that gives the poem a catechism or ballad-dialogue quality. The questioner asks the poet what he needs; the poet answers in botanical terms ('many songs / From a little thought-seed: / Blossoming myriads, / Flower and weed'); the questioner then delivers the brutal rejoinder: 'Drink, lust, starvation, / By these you are freed.' 'Meed' is an archaic word for reward or recompense (from Old English *mēd*), still current in early twentieth-century poetry; its archaism sharpens the irony. The poem's argument -- that the price of artistic productivity is self-destruction -- belongs to the Romantic-Decadent mythology of the doomed artist, from Chatterton and Keats through Rimbaud's deliberate derangement of the senses.
