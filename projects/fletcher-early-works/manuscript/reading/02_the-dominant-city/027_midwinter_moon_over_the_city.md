## Original Lineation

The tarnished moon spins upward like a piece
Cast by some starving loafer on a bar,
To buy that poison which may give release
An instant, from the weight of things that are.

'Mid the grey chill which makes the blood run
  slow,
'Mid intellectual women coarse and cold,
'Mid sentimental joy and silly woe,
'Mid all the misery of a land grown old:

'Mid thin black clouds like ragged strips of crêpe
Upon a pauper's hearse, she makes her way;
Bound to a wheel from which is no escape,
Towards the dull grey mockery of a day.

A little nearer, then, that frozen death
Which will conclude our childish discontent
'Gainst grim endurance of each painful breath
Which, to this land of tears, the gods have sent.

## Context Notes

'Crêpe' (l. 9) is the black silk or worsted fabric draped on hearses in mourning; a pauper's hearse would use cheaper stuff — the word is both economically precise and tonally perfect. Ll. 11–12 ('Bound to a wheel from which is no escape, / Towards the dull grey mockery of a day'): the wheel is both the lunar orbit and the wheel of Ixion in classical myth — Ixion being bound eternally to a spinning wheel in Tartarus as punishment for his crimes against the gods.

Four pentameter quatrains rhymed abab (sixteen lines, not a sonnet); the 'Mid anaphora of the middle two stanzas does the structural work that an octave's logic would have done.
