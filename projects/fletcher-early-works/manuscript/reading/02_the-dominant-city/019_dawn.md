## Original Lineation

The sun rends the long veils of smoke, and the
  fogs
Of night shift and vanish in upper air:
While the eternal huddle of chimneys now
To the light pour forth their praise from yawning
  mouths.

Blackened with grief, blistered with lust of life,
Unconquerable and unconquered, they cast their
  spell:
Crowding with vague forms the infinite emptiness
Of the pale seas of air that stretch out from dawn
  to dark.

Now man awakes to another day, to a life
That has forgotten its past,--that must forget all
  days to come.
Until the incontrollable impulse is exhausted,
Until no more feet sound, no more voices shake
  with desire,
Towards the illusion circle-wise he treads
Along the track of ancient unreality.

## Editorial Relineation

The sun rends the long veils of smoke, and the fogs
Of night shift and vanish in upper air:
While the eternal huddle of chimneys now
To the light pour forth their praise from yawning mouths.

Blackened with grief, blistered with lust of life,
Unconquerable and unconquered, they cast their spell:
Crowding with vague forms the infinite emptiness
Of the pale seas of air that stretch out from dawn to dark.

Now man awakes to another day, to a life
That has forgotten its past,--that must forget all days to come.
Until the incontrollable impulse is exhausted,
Until no more feet sound, no more voices shake with desire,
Towards the illusion circle-wise he treads
Along the track of ancient unreality.

## Context Notes

Baudelaire closes the *Tableaux parisiens* with 'Le Crépuscule du matin,' where dawn advances over a deserted Paris and the city, rubbing its eyes, takes up its tools like an old man. Fletcher's chimneys (ll. 3–4, 'pour forth their praise from yawning mouths') arrive at the same conclusion: dawn not as renewal but as resumption of the sentence — l. 9 ('man awakes to another day, to a life / That has forgotten its past').

Free accentual verse in three strophes of four, four, and six lines, unrhymed; the strophes lengthen as the day's sentence resumes.
