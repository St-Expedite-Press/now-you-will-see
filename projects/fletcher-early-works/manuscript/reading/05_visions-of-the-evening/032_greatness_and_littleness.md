## Original Lineation

How many million souls, through all these
    years
Now well-forgotten, fought their way
    through life.
And suffering, poured out sweat and blood and tears,
Hard pressed by fate, in base or noble strife.
All these that ate each day, slept well at night,
Ploughed field, sailed ship, reared many a lofty wall,
Even their graves have vanished now from sight,
To us they might have never lived at all.
So Littleness in mighty stream o'erwhelmed
Their little stream of Might; and even They,
Saints, Poets, Prophets, Warriors mailed and
    helmed,
Achieved how much in life during Their day?
Because of them no pulse the faster starts:
They too are failures, aye, and broken hearts.

## Context Notes

A Shakespearean sonnet (ABAB CDCD EFEF GG), the poem extends the meditation on historical oblivion from poem 031 to a direct catalogue of the forgotten: 'All these that ate each day, slept well at night, / Ploughed field, sailed ship, reared many a lofty wall, / Even their graves have vanished now from sight.' 'Mailed and helmed' means armoured and helmeted (mail being chainmail armour, the helm being a helmet): the phrasing gives the military saints and warriors a medieval quality. The sonnet's final couplet -- 'They too are failures, aye, and broken hearts' -- is the collection's bleakest formulation of the failure-thesis: not just the obscure millions but 'Saints, Poets, Prophets, Warriors' are all failures, equally forgotten, equally unable to make anyone's 'pulse the faster start.' The argument echoes Shelley's 'Ozymandias' (1818) but without the implicit consolation that the artist's record endures.
