## Original Lineation

My heart is drunken with an old dance of
    swords,
Played by black pipers in innumerable
    hordes,
At dawning of a flapping, wringing day,
Where the red rowan flares upon the grey.
My heart is drunken with an old dance of swords.

My heart is maddened with a wild dance of war;
With keen blades flickering, flickering afar:
And desperate flights heartbreaking, chill, and sore,
Over the treeless, houseless, puddled moor:
In the mist there go click of swords and cries of
    war:--
My heart is broken by that old dance of war.

My heart is weary of a black dance of swords:
Far off there skirl the shrieking pipes, the hordes
Of pipers vanish, far off over the snow.
O, I am weary, weary, of dull woe:
I would have done with sorrow and with words.
My heart is weary of a black dance of swords.

## Editorial Relineation

My heart is drunken with an old dance of swords,
Played by black pipers in innumerable hordes,
At dawning of a flapping, wringing day,
Where the red rowan flares upon the grey.
My heart is drunken with an old dance of swords.

My heart is maddened with a wild dance of war;
With keen blades flickering, flickering afar:
And desperate flights heartbreaking, chill, and sore,
Over the treeless, houseless, puddled moor:
In the mist there go click of swords and cries of war:--
My heart is broken by that old dance of war.

My heart is weary of a black dance of swords:
Far off there skirl the shrieking pipes, the hordes
Of pipers vanish, far off over the snow.
O, I am weary, weary, of dull woe:
I would have done with sorrow and with words.
My heart is weary of a black dance of swords.

## Context Notes

Three quintains, each closing with a refrain that transforms across stanzas: 'drunken' (l. 5) → 'broken' (l. 11) → 'weary' (l. 17), tracing the arc from intoxication to exhaustion.

l. 4 ('red rowan'): *Sorbus aucuparia*, the rowan tree; its berries turn red in autumn and winter and are strongly associated with the Scottish Highlands.

l. 13 ('skirl'): The specific verb for the sharp, shrill sound of bagpipes, from Old Norse *skvala* (to shriek); the 'old dance of swords' (ll. 1, 11, 17) is also the Gillie Callum, the traditional Highland sword dance performed over crossed blades.
