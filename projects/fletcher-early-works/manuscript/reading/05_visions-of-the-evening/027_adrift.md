## Original Lineation

The mist hangs low on the banks
    Of the northern sea,
And the mountains in long ranks
Stand silently:
Out of the south to the north
    Their shadows cast,
Facing the west, leap forth,
    To the waters glassed.

There is nor life nor grave
    On the northern sea,
Only the bitter wave
    Apart, for me:--
And the long mountain glades
    Where, set on high,
A shadowy ruin fades
    Into the sky.

The fierce suns of midday
    Long have withered my heart;
My face is wet with the spray,
    My hands have forgotten their art.
Now in the evening grave
    Which darkens fast,
Grey ghosts seem to slip from each wave
    As it shudders past.

## Context Notes

Three octets in ABABCDCD.

l. 20 ('My hands have forgotten their art'): A parallel to poem 009 ('I Contemplate My Toil'), where the creative act has become a dead weight rather than a lost capacity; here it is simply absent.

ll. 21–23 ('Now in the evening grave / Which darkens fast, / Grey ghosts seem to slip from each wave'): Evening figured as a grave; the northern sea populated by anonymous spectral dead.
