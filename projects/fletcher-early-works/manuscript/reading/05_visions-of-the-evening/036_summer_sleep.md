## Original Lineation

We will sleep in the high-pillared pavilions
    of late summer nights,
Watching the mists take vague and altering
    reflections
From the crimson lanterns, the towering tripods of
    flame,
Set all around the hall, overflowing with golden
    lights.

We will slumber, or we will drink while the crimson-
    robed, dark-eyed dance-girls
Weave a few wayward paces 'mid the cups and
    flowers on the floor:
Perhaps wine, passion, idleness, pass too; but then,
    what matter?
They may last for a moment, who knows? We are
    young yet, and they are ours.

Forget then the past, 'tis a sorry song soon sung:
Forget too the future that follows, forget that time
    moves ever on.
Let us sleep in the high-pillared pavilions of late
    summer nights;
Watching the moon's blue breath on the mirror of
    the pond.

## Editorial Relineation

We will sleep in the high-pillared pavilions of late summer nights,
Watching the mists take vague and altering reflections
From the crimson lanterns, the towering tripods of flame,
Set all around the hall, overflowing with golden lights.

We will slumber, or we will drink while the crimson-robed, dark-eyed dance-girls
Weave a few wayward paces 'mid the cups and flowers on the floor:
Perhaps wine, passion, idleness, pass too; but then, what matter?
They may last for a moment, who knows? We are young yet, and they are ours.

Forget then the past, 'tis a sorry song soon sung:
Forget too the future that follows, forget that time moves ever on.
Let us sleep in the high-pillared pavilions of late summer nights;
Watching the moon's blue breath on the mirror of the pond.

## Context Notes

Three stanzas in loose hexameter quatrains with an editorial relineation, the poem is an Orientalist fantasy of sybaritic ease -- 'high-pillared pavilions,' 'crimson lanterns,' 'towering tripods of flame,' 'crimson-robed, dark-eyed dance-girls' -- that draws on the same tradition as *Fools' Gold*'s 'Life' (poem 008) and *Visions of the Evening*'s 'The Valley of Kashmir' (poem 006). The 'tripods of flame' are bronze braziers on three legs, a form associated with Classical Greek and Chinese ceremonial contexts. FitzGerald's *Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám* (1859) is the obvious template: 'A Book of Verses underneath the Bough, / A Jug of Wine, a Loaf of Bread -- and Thou.' The poem's carpe diem argument -- 'forget the past... forget that time moves ever on' -- is unusual in a collection so dominated by regret and retrospection; the plural 'we' again suggests a shared ideal rather than a solitary vision.
