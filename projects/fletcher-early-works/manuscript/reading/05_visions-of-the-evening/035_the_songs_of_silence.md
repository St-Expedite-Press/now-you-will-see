## Original Lineation

These are the songs of silence my heart keeps
Within the city's tumult-stirring deeps:
In the dark attentive forest the flight of a bird;
A low sharp flap that seems more felt than heard;
Or in sunlit slumbering villages the long cry
From a pedlar's creaking cart which drowses by;
Or the brief rustling of the wind that fills
With a vague movement, the low hollow-flanked
    hills;
Or the sudden amazement of the slithered rain
Tossed idly by a gust upon the pane;
Or the broken vibrations of twilight in summer long
Upon old streets where no one lifts a song;
Or anything which passes, melts or fades.
Clouds within which, locked in dim amber shades,
Dark thunder and pale lightning sleep embraced:
Atmosphere's opal sea which is enchased
With sunlight's long thin threads, blue shafts of
    rain;
And the distance which seems to reflect, again and
    again,
In a twilight manner, the too-real things near by,
These are the songs of silence from on high
Descending to my soul, deluded one,
And each is an angel bearing a separate sun;
Which blinds my eyes so that they only see
What is unseen and what can never be:
On solitude's veil the grey embroidery.

## Context Notes

Thirty-six lines in a single extended free-verse period, one of the most Impressionist poems in all of Fletcher's early work, the poem catalogues the 'songs of silence' -- paradoxically, sounds that are perceived as silence against the city's noise: a bird's flight felt more than heard, a pedlar's cart cry, wind in hollow hills, rain on a windowpane, twilight vibrations in old streets. 'Enchased' (engraved or inlaid) echoes poem 026 in *Fire and Wine* ('The Three Transformations of Poetry'), where the sword of poetry is 'enchased'; here the atmosphere's 'opal sea' is enchased with 'sunlight's long thin threads, blue shafts of rain.' 'Opal sea' gives the sky a gemstone quality: opal is a silica mineral that shows iridescent colour-play (*opalescence*). The closing triad -- 'angels bearing a separate sun' that blinds the speaker, showing only 'what is unseen and what can never be: / On solitude's veil the grey embroidery' -- returns to the collection's governing trope of solitude as the medium through which reality is perceived.
