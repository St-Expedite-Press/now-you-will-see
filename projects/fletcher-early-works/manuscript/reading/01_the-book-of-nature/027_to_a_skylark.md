## Original Lineation

O thou unwearied one, tread the dance of the sun,
On a height of great wonder and of wild delight
To the rhythm of the day and the mad wind's sway.

Not a thrush with his warning shall thy note ever
  hush
With its ecstasy profound, rippling, bubbling
  wells of sound:--
Silence now that meditation that he mutters from
  his bush,

Like a Buddhist monk a-dreaming of Nirvana afar!
Let thy wings and thy feet and thy voice together
  greet
The grassy plain's great bound, the skies' blue
  profound,

That at its wooded edge weaves a vast circle round.
Like a rocket in its flight, a balloon, or a kite,
In the rhythm of thy voice now rejoice.

## Editorial Relineation

O thou unwearied one, tread the dance of the sun,
On a height of great wonder and of wild delight
To the rhythm of the day and the mad wind's sway.

Not a thrush with his warning shall thy note ever hush
With its ecstasy profound, rippling, bubbling wells of sound:--
Silence now that meditation that he mutters from his bush,

Like a Buddhist monk a-dreaming of Nirvana afar!
Let thy wings and thy feet and thy voice together greet
The grassy plain's great bound, the skies' blue profound,

That at its wooded edge weaves a vast circle round.
Like a rocket in its flight, a balloon, or a kite,
In the rhythm of thy voice now rejoice.

## Context Notes

The title deliberately invokes Shelley's 'To a Skylark' — 'Hail to thee, blithe Spirit! / Bird thou never wert' — and then refuses the premise. Shelley's lark is bodiless spirit; Fletcher's is all body, urged to let 'thy wings and thy feet and thy voice together greet' the plain (l. 9), and to out-sing the thrush who meditates 'Like a Buddhist monk a-dreaming of Nirvana afar' (l. 7). Where the Romantic bird dissolves into joy, this one is compared to 'a rocket in its flight, a balloon, or a kite' (l. 11) — machinery of lift, not transcendence. Four anapestic tercets with end-rhymes drifting across stanza boundaries ('sound... profound... round').
