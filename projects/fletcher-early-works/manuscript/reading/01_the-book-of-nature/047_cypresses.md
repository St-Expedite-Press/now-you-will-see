## Original Lineation

Cypresses motionless, double-rank arrayed,
  Knights clad in tarnished bronze and silver
  mail:
Funereal plumes nod on each towering head,
  Fluting, the breezes low and solemn wail.

Tunes tinged with delicate, fastidious old romance,
  Elegies of pride, of passion-crimson lust,
Whispers of gallantry and ancient dalliance,
  Quiver now amid them, shaking out their dust.

Weedy dim pathways wind amid their stems,
  Leading to pleasure-courts of long and lazy
  hours,
Where from old fountains, crowned with diadems,
  Into green basins languid water pours.

Here are carven trophies, antique helms and spears,
  Like to these cypresses in a long line cast.
Masks too, grotesque with rage or twisted leers,
  Mock with their hollow mouths the splendour
  of the past.

Winds from the bare brown hazy hills awake
  And, half laughing, scratch the heads of these
  old trees.
Solemn as bearded gods, the sunburnt summits
  shake,
Counselling age and endless rest and peace.

## Context Notes

A poem about cypress trees -- the tall dark Mediterranean evergreens (*Cupressus sempervirens*) planted along garden allées and cemetery paths since antiquity, sacred to Pluto and associated with mourning. Fletcher personifies them as a line of armoured knights ('double-rank arrayed, / Knights clad in tarnished bronze and silver mail') with funereal plumes nodding, and the sound of wind through them as 'Elegies of pride, of passion-crimson lust.' 'Diadems' are crowns; the crowned fountain-basins recall formal Italian garden design. 'Antique helms and spears' and 'Masks too, grotesque with rage or twisted leers' are features of the classical Italian garden grotesque decoration tradition -- carved stone theatrical masks, trophy-pieces. The five-stanza poem in ABAB quatrains with a closing couplet (six-line stanzas: ABABCC) echoes the stanza of Keats's 'Ode to a Nightingale,' appropriate for a meditation on beautiful, funereal sound.

Form: five quatrains in long iambic lines, pentameter stretching toward hexameter, rhymed abab. The note above finds a six-line Keatsian stanza here; the page shows quatrains.
