## Original Lineation

Autumn's heavy galleon-hosts
  Rock and reel on the stormy main
Of the chilly sky.  By rocky coasts
  They hurl bombardments of dark rain:
The wind, a swaggering roisterer,
On their flank has roused a stir.

Through their decks in a cataract
  Rush his bold blaspheming tones:
Wealth of all the Indies sacked,
  Weight of golden leaves at once,
Hurriedly he strips away
In his desperate foray.

Some he bears, the buccaneer,
  To a favoured cranny hid
Chartless, rocky shoals amid,
  Where he buries them each year.
But the most away he flings
As he staggers on and sings.

Far behind him the cloud's swift
  Men-o'-war and brigantines
On the skyline slowly lift
  Ponderous sail, in long dark lines:
And from out their straining sides
Leaps the grey smoke of broadsides.

## Context Notes

The extended metaphor of the autumn wind as a pirate or buccaneer plundering autumn leaves as 'golden treasure' is sustained across four stanzas, each using the vocabulary of naval warfare: 'galleon-hosts,' 'bombardments,' 'roisterer,' 'bold blaspheming,' 'buccaneer,' 'brigantines,' 'men-o'-war,' 'broadsides.' 'Men-o'-war' are heavily armed warships; 'brigantines' are two-masted sailing vessels often associated in the nineteenth century with piracy. The clouds as a pursuing naval squadron in the final stanza give the poem a mock-epic dimension: the small-scale domestic fact of leaves blowing into corners is rendered in the rhetoric of an Elizabethan sea-battle. The six-line stanzas in alternating rhyme (ABABCC) are the form of the Elizabethan narrative stanza, appropriate to the swashbuckling subject.

Form: four tetrameter sixains rhymed ababcc -- save the third, which folds inward to abbacc exactly where the buccaneer buries his hoard. The sixain is the Venus-and-Adonis stanza scaled down from pentameter.
