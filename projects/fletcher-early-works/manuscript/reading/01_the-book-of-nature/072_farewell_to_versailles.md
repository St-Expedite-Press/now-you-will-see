## Original Lineation

Farewell, O dim horizons of the past,
Multitudinous mirages that could not last!
You were as white swans sailing through
A basin's cool profound of blue,
Or towers of white cloud blotted out
Swiftly, by a storm rack's rout.
Farewell, pursued but flying hours:
I shall dream to-night beneath snow or flowers.

Farewell, futility!  I may not tell
To thee, alas! my last farewell:
Farewell, O alleys infinite,
That drew outward this my sight.
Farewell, chill winter winds that range,
Sun-drunken summers, green and strange;
Farewell, you scarlet or opal hours:
I shall dream to-night beneath snow or flowers.

## Context Notes

A two-stanza poem with a refrain line repeated at the close of each stanza: 'I shall dream to-night beneath snow or flowers.' The structure is rondel-like (an Old French fixed form in which the opening line or couplet returns as a refrain), though Fletcher uses it freely rather than mechanically. 'Storm rack' is 'storm-rack' or 'wrack': driving clouds or debris carried by wind, a nautical and atmospheric term used in Elizabethan poetry (Shakespeare, Milton). The 'dim horizons of the past, / Multitudinous mirages that could not last' are the French landscape dissolving as Fletcher prepares to leave Versailles. 'Alleys infinite' are the famous long straight garden allées (tree-lined paths) of Versailles that appear to recede to vanishing points. The final 'snow or flowers' holds together two seasonal extremes (winter and spring) as equally possible dreams -- the poem refuses to predict which season will follow the Versailles autumn.

Form: two eight-line stanzas in couplets, four- and five-beat lines, each stanza closing on the refrain 'I shall dream to-night beneath snow or flowers' -- the rondel manner the note above describes, without the rondel's bookkeeping.
