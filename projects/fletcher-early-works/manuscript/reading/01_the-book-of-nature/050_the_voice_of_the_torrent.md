## Original Lineation

It is the voice of the torrent that between its iron-
  black crags
Rends its own beauty asunder, to innumerable
  splinters and jags,
Lacing itself with white scars of foam in its mad-
  ness to reach its rest.
It is the voice of the chasm:--'O Sun, be thou
  ever blest,
That drew me up and in lightning-sent vapours
  new-fashioned me;
That hurried me on in black hordes from the lip of
  thy sea
In a myriad self-destructive migrations towards
  the peak afar,
That hurled me to darkness upon a frozen scaur
In thy superb everlasting death-struggle with
  Death;
That receivest now my hoarse monotonous breath,
O Baal, and the smoke and the blown spray of
  sacrifice!
Take all of these which are thine where the black
  pines rise,
As thou tak'st in the South that life which thou
  promisest me,
That peace of thy sultry passionate summer sea.'

## Context Notes

A dramatic monologue in which a mountain torrent speaks its own mythology. 'Baal' (also 'Ba'al') is a Semitic deity title meaning 'lord' or 'master,' used for several Canaanite storm and sun gods; the Old Testament repeatedly condemns Israelite worship of Baal as apostasy. Fletcher's torrent calls the sun 'O Baal,' placing itself in a primordial sacrificial relationship -- spray and sound as 'smoke and the blown spray of sacrifice' -- that draws on the ancient association between fire (sun) and water (river) in fertility religion. A 'scaur' (also 'scar') is a steep rocky cliff-face, common in northern English dialect (Lake District, Yorkshire Dales): the word places the alpine torrent in both a Swiss/Italian and a northern English landscape simultaneously. The poem's long alexandrine-like lines with internal rhymes and the torrent's extended address creates an effect of rushing, inescapable momentum.

Form: fourteen long anapestic lines in couplets -- a sonnet's count without a sonnet's conduct. Three lines of frame, then the chasm speaks to the end.
