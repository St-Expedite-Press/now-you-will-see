## Original Lineation

It is evening, and I rest beneath the pines.
Through their meshes a star in the grey-blue sky
Twinkles: the saffron robes of departing day
Are trailed down the western skies in melancholy
  guise:
A voice is singing to me over and over again:--
'My garments are woven of the dark-green fronds
  of the pine;
I am brief trembling joy and sorrow frail and
  forlorn.
I shake about me the mystery of my hair,
Fragrant with shadowy sighs and rustling with
  myriads of tears.'

## Context Notes

The pine tree as a voice of mysterious feminine sorrow is a motif Fletcher inherits from multiple traditions: the syrinx of Greek myth (the nymph Syrinx transformed into hollow reeds that Pan then fashioned into a pipe), Swinburne's 'The Garden of Proserpine' (where the pine-voices are voices of the dead), and the French Symbolist tradition of the forest as a network of whispering correspondences (Verlaine's 'Il pleure dans mon coeur'). The voice's self-description -- 'My garments are woven of the dark-green fronds of the pine; / I am brief trembling joy and sorrow frail and forlorn' -- positions the tree-voice as a dryad or Hamadryad (a wood-nymph whose life is bound to a particular tree) speaking her own nature. The poem's free verse with its varied line lengths enacts the irregular gusts of a pine forest at dusk.

Form: free verse, nine lines -- five of scene, then the voice's four-line song in quotation marks, the cadence lengthening once the voice begins.
