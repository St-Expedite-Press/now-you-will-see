## Original Lineation

## I

The world's a great pain-maddened tragic dance,
Wherein grey satyrs, withered maenads prance:
So do not yield to nobler things your soul,
But drain the grape lees lurking in the bowl.
Indifferent to to-day's, to-morrow's fate,
Now let the frost upon the wine-cup wait:
And waste 'mid autumn's downward-shuddering
  leaves,
The last of summer's heavy harvest sheaves.
The cups to their old spell our hearts have strung,
Unrevered wrinkles look now almost young;
Let us to our last bacchanal advance:
The world's a great pain-maddened tragic dance!

## II

The leaves gyrate in fury round and round;
They harshly rattle with a dry thin sound.
'Tis like cracked evil laughter, shrill as sin,
Of chattering witches at the devil's inn.

These are my songless days, not fled away
But blown about me in the death of day:
Blown by the wind that chills my lagging feet,
And stings my face with frozen tears of sleet.

Ironically old memories circling whirl
About my brain like this brown withered swirl;
No more my sun burns on the peaks of gold,
Dark rooms and firelight loves the heart that's old:

Let loose upon the land the grey sharp blast,
And blow my leaves away from first to last!
For they are dead, and I am dead as well--
A dry leaf whirled about a sunless hell.

The leaves gyrate in fury round and round;
They harshly rattle with a dry thin sound.

## Context Notes

A two-part poem: Part I is a twelve-line rondeau-like form ending by repeating its opening line ('The world's a great pain-maddened tragic dance'), a structure recalling Villon's ballades and their envoi; Part II, 'The Leaves,' is a ten-line lyric with an AABB refrain structure, framing a meditation on dead leaves as dead days. 'Satyrs' and 'maenads' in Part I are figures from Greek Dionysiac worship: satyrs are part-human, part-goat woodland spirits of animal appetite; maenads (literally 'mad women') were the ecstatic female worshippers of Dionysus who danced until possession by the god. In Part II the speaker identifies himself with the dead leaves: 'I am dead as well -- / A dry leaf whirled about a sunless hell.' This self-identification with autumn debris was a Decadent commonplace (Verlaine, 'Chanson d'automne': 'les sanglots longs / Des violons / De l'automne'), but Fletcher's version is more raw, less musicalized.

Form: two movements in pentameter couplets. I is a twelve-line ring closed by the return of its own first line; II runs four couplet-quatrains framed fore and aft by a recurring couplet -- eighteen lines, not the ten counted above. The ring and the frame do the whirling the leaves are said to do.
