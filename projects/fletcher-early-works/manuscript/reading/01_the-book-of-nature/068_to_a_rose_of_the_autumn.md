## Original Lineation

Late rose that lingerest strangely in old gardens,
Amid the sombre splendours of the autumn,
What dost thou here, flushed crimson mouth of
  passion?
The laughter has fled: the dancers have de-
  parted;
There are no breezes to kiss thee; here dost solicit
  in vain
Love by the wind-ruffled pools, where no flung
  jets from the fountains
Aspire to the pale, cool-mottled, windy, and chilly
  sky.
Of blossoms of love art the last: see, the mad
  dead leaves dance about thee!
Soon thy translucent ruby vase shall be swayed,
  cracked, and shattered:
And thy petals shall drift over the fresh-fallen
  snow.

## Editorial Relineation

Late rose that lingerest strangely in old gardens,
Amid the sombre splendours of the autumn,
What dost thou here, flushed crimson mouth of passion?
The laughter has fled: the dancers have departed;
There are no breezes to kiss thee; here dost solicit in vain
Love by the wind-ruffled pools, where no flung jets from the fountains
Aspire to the pale, cool-mottled, windy, and chilly sky.
Of blossoms of love art the last: see, the mad dead leaves dance about thee!
Soon thy translucent ruby vase shall be swayed, cracked, and shattered:
And thy petals shall drift over the fresh-fallen snow.

## Context Notes

An apostrophe to a late autumn rose still blooming in the Versailles gardens -- 'the wind-ruffled pools, where no flung jets from the fountains / Aspire to the pale, cool-mottled, windy, and chilly sky' tells us the fountain jets have been turned off for the season, another precise detail of Versailles in late October. The address to the rose as 'flushed crimson mouth of passion' and 'Of blossoms of love art the last' belongs to the tradition of the *alba* and Romantic ode to an ephemeral beauty; Keats's 'To Autumn' has the late flowers in the 'half-reap'd furrow,' the last warmth on stubble-plains. The final image -- 'thy petals shall drift over the fresh-fallen snow' -- gives the rose a posthumous dispersal that is both botanically accurate (petals persisting after the first frost) and elegiacally appropriate.

Form: ten unrhymed long lines -- the companion piece to 'To a Rose of the Trianon,' same address, same refusal of rhyme; the lines swell toward the middle of the poem and die back to the short last.
