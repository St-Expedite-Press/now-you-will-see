## Original Lineation

I lay beside a fountain, listening in the shadow
To a murmur of far-off voices that faded and
  then restirred:
And it seemed to me that a great song of passion
  and praise they were making,
Afar where I could scarce hear them, for the
  wind tossed their singing away.
Around me were lichened benches covered with
  flower-petals;
Fallen and withering petals showered from
  stooping trees,
Trailing green locks in the pool, as they gazed at
  their reflections,
Through the thick tangles of their tousled hair.
And there were pedestals, but lacking statues;
And there were naiads moulded in greenish
  bronze:
I lay beside the fountain and I tossed up flower-
  petals
Into the air, but marked not the spot whereon
  they fell:
Till the fountain tossed up too its filmy veil of
  water,
That shook the air and shattered the murmurs
  beyond the leaves.

## Editorial Relineation

I lay beside a fountain, listening in the shadow
To a murmur of far-off voices that faded and then restirred:
And it seemed to me that a great song of passion and praise they were making,
Afar where I could scarce hear them, for the wind tossed their singing away.
Around me were lichened benches covered with flower-petals;
Fallen and withering petals showered from stooping trees,
Trailing green locks in the pool, as they gazed at their reflections,
Through the thick tangles of their tousled hair.
And there were pedestals, but lacking statues;
And there were naiads moulded in greenish bronze:
I lay beside the fountain and I tossed up flower-petals
Into the air, but marked not the spot whereon they fell:
Till the fountain tossed up too its filmy veil of water,
That shook the air and shattered the murmurs beyond the leaves.

## Context Notes

Set in the Versailles gardens, this poem describes the ornamental fountains and their bronze figures ('naiads moulded in greenish bronze'). Naiads are water-nymphs of Greek myth who inhabit rivers, springs, and fountains; the royal gardens of Versailles are populated with bronze mythological figures, including water-nymphs at fountain basins. The poem's setting -- 'lichened benches,' 'pedestals, but lacking statues,' stooping trees trailing in a pool -- is a vision of the gardens in decay or at least neglect, an autumnal Versailles. The speaker's idle gesture of tossing flower-petals without marking where they fall is a small emblem of the collection's persistent theme of purposeless beauty.

Form: fourteen unrhymed long lines -- the sonnet's count held loosely, as in 'The Voice of the Torrent' earlier in this Part; cadence by paragraph, not stanza.
